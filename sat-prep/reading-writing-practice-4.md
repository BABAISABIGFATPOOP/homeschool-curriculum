### Questions 7–9

The following passage is from a 2023 article on quantum computing.

Quantum computers leverage the principles of quantum mechanics to process information in fundamentally different ways than classical computers. While classical bits exist in one of two states — 0 or 1 — quantum bits, or qubits, can exist in a "superposition" of both states simultaneously. This property, combined with quantum entanglement (in which the state of one qubit is correlated with another regardless of distance), allows quantum computers to evaluate many possible solutions in parallel. For certain classes of problems, such as factoring large numbers, simulating molecular interactions, and optimizing complex logistics, quantum computers could theoretically solve in minutes what would take classical supercomputers millions of years. However, quantum computing faces formidable engineering challenges. Qubits are extraordinarily fragile, losing their quantum properties through a process called "decoherence" when they interact with their environment. Current quantum computers require cooling to temperatures near absolute zero and can maintain coherent qubit states for only microseconds to milliseconds. Error correction, which in classical computing is straightforward, requires exponentially more physical qubits than logical qubits in quantum systems, meaning that a practical, fault-tolerant quantum computer may require millions of physical qubits — far beyond today's machines, which operate with a few hundred to a few thousand.
