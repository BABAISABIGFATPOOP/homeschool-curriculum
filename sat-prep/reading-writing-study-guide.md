# SAT Reading and Writing Complete Study Guide

## Overview

The SAT Reading and Writing section contains 54 questions across two 32-minute modules (27 questions each). The section is adaptive: the difficulty of the second module depends on your performance in the first module.

Unlike the old SAT, the Digital SAT uses short passages (typically 25-150 words) with one question per passage. This means you need to read quickly, identify the key point, and answer precisely.

The four content domains are:
1. **Craft and Structure** (~28% of questions)
2. **Information and Ideas** (~26% of questions)
3. **Standard English Conventions** (~26% of questions)
4. **Expression of Ideas** (~20% of questions)

---

## Domain 1: Craft and Structure

### Words in Context

**What it tests:** Your ability to determine the meaning of a word or phrase as it is used in a specific passage.

**Strategy:**
1. Read the passage and identify the word being tested
2. Cover the word and think about what word would make sense in context
3. Look for the answer choice that best matches the meaning you predicted
4. Plug your choice back in to confirm it makes sense

**Common traps:**
- The most common or familiar definition of a word may not be the correct one in context
- Look for connotation (positive, negative, neutral) as well as denotation

**Example passage:**
"The senator's proposal, while ambitious in scope, was met with considerable skepticism by her colleagues, who found its projected costs to be untenable."

**Question:** As used in the passage, "untenable" most nearly means:
A) Indefensible
B) Unsustainable
C) Unreasonable
D) Impossible

**Answer:** B — In the context of costs, "untenable" means they cannot be sustained or maintained.

### Text Structure and Purpose

**What it tests:** Your ability to identify why an author wrote a passage or how a passage is organized.

**Question types:**
- "Which choice best describes the function of the underlined sentence?"
- "Which choice best states the main purpose of the text?"
- "How does the author structure the passage?"

**Common text structures:**
- **Cause and effect:** explains why something happens
- **Compare and contrast:** shows similarities and differences
- **Problem and solution:** presents a problem and proposes a fix
- **Chronological:** presents events in time order
- **Claim and evidence:** makes an argument supported by evidence

**Strategy:**
1. Identify the main topic in the first sentence or two
2. Determine what the author does with that topic (describe, argue, compare, explain, etc.)
3. Match the overall purpose to the answer choices
4. Avoid answers that focus on minor details rather than the passage as a whole

### Cross-Text Connections

**What it tests:** Your ability to understand how two short texts relate to each other.

**You will see two short passages** and be asked how the authors' views relate.

**Common relationships:**
- Text 2 supports or strengthens Text 1's claim
- Text 2 challenges or undermines Text 1's claim
- Text 2 qualifies or adds a condition to Text 1's claim
- Both texts agree on one point but disagree on another
- Text 2 offers an alternative explanation to the same phenomenon

**Strategy:**
1. Read Text 1 and identify its main claim
2. Read Text 2 and identify its main claim
3. Determine the relationship: agreement, disagreement, qualification, or extension
4. Choose the answer that accurately describes this relationship

## Domain 2: Information and Ideas

### Central Ideas and Details

**What it tests:** Your ability to identify the main idea of a passage or a key supporting detail.

**Question types:**
- "Which choice best states the main idea of the text?"
- "According to the passage, which of the following is true about...?"
- "Based on the text, what is the primary reason...?"

**Strategy for main idea questions:**
1. Read the entire passage (do not stop after the first sentence)
2. Ask yourself: "What is this passage mainly about?"
3. The main idea should cover the whole passage, not just one part
4. Eliminate answers that are too narrow (only about one detail) or too broad (beyond what the passage discusses)

**Strategy for detail questions:**
1. These are fact-finding missions — the answer is stated directly in the text
2. Read the relevant part of the passage carefully
3. The correct answer is a faithful paraphrase of what the text says
4. Avoid answers that distort, exaggerate, or go beyond what the text states

### Command of Evidence (Textual)

**What it tests:** Your ability to select a quotation or detail that best supports a given claim.

**Question type:** "Which quotation from the passage most effectively illustrates the claim?"

**Strategy:**
1. Read the claim carefully
2. For each answer choice, ask: "Does this directly support the claim?"
3. The best evidence is specific, relevant, and directly linked to the claim
4. Avoid choices that are tangential or only loosely related

### Command of Evidence (Quantitative)

**What it tests:** Your ability to read data from tables, charts, and graphs and use that data to support or complete a statement.

**Question type:** "Which choice most effectively uses data from the table to complete the statement?"

**Strategy:**
1. Read the statement or claim carefully — know what you are looking for
2. Examine the data display: read titles, labels, axes, and units
3. Identify the specific data point(s) that relate to the claim
4. Choose the answer that accurately represents what the data shows
5. Be precise — watch for common traps like confusing rows/columns or misreading scale

### Inferences

**What it tests:** Your ability to draw a logical conclusion based on information in the passage.

**Question type:** "Based on the text, which of the following can most reasonably be inferred?"

**Strategy:**
1. An inference must be supported by the text — it is not a wild guess
2. The correct inference follows logically and directly from what is stated
3. Avoid answers that require assumptions not supported by the passage
4. The best inference is usually modest and closely tied to the text
5. If you have to make a big leap to reach a conclusion, it is probably wrong

## Domain 3: Standard English Conventions

### Sentence Structure

**Complete sentences** must have a subject and a verb and express a complete thought.

**Run-on sentences (comma splices):**
- WRONG: "The experiment was successful, the results were published."
- FIX: Use a period, semicolon, or a comma with a conjunction
  - "The experiment was successful. The results were published."
  - "The experiment was successful; the results were published."
  - "The experiment was successful, and the results were published."

**Sentence fragments:**
- WRONG: "Because the experiment was successful." (dependent clause alone)
- FIX: Attach to an independent clause or remove the subordinating word
  - "Because the experiment was successful, the results were published."
  - "The experiment was successful."

**Parallel structure:**
- Items in a list or comparison should have the same grammatical form
- WRONG: "She likes swimming, running, and to bike."
- RIGHT: "She likes swimming, running, and biking."

### Subject-Verb Agreement

**The verb must agree with the subject in number (singular or plural).**

**Tricky situations:**
1. **Words between subject and verb:** "The box of chocolates **is** on the table." (subject is "box," not "chocolates")
2. **Inverted sentences:** "There **are** many reasons to study." (subject is "reasons")
3. **Compound subjects with "and":** "The dog and the cat **are** playing." (plural)
4. **Compound subjects with "or/nor":** Verb agrees with the closer subject. "Neither the students nor the teacher **was** ready."
5. **Collective nouns:** Usually singular. "The team **is** winning." "The committee **has** decided."
6. **Indefinite pronouns:**
   - Always singular: each, every, everyone, somebody, nobody, either, neither
   - Always plural: both, few, many, several
   - Depends on context: some, all, none, most, any

### Pronoun Agreement and Reference

**Pronouns must agree with their antecedent in number, gender, and person.**

- WRONG: "Every student should bring their book." (formally — "every" is singular)
- RIGHT: "Every student should bring his or her book." or "All students should bring their books."

**Pronoun clarity:**
- WRONG: "When John met Bob, he was nervous." (who was nervous?)
- RIGHT: "When John met Bob, John was nervous."

**Pronoun case:**
- Subject pronouns: I, he, she, we, they, who
- Object pronouns: me, him, her, us, them, whom
- Possessive: my, his, her, our, their, whose

**Tip for "who" vs. "whom":** Substitute "he/she" (who) or "him/her" (whom). "Who did this?" = "He did this." "To whom did you give it?" = "You gave it to him."

### Verb Tense

**Keep tenses consistent** unless there is a clear reason to shift.

**Key tenses:**
- **Simple present:** habitual actions or general truths ("Water boils at 100°C.")
- **Simple past:** completed actions ("She finished the test yesterday.")
- **Present perfect:** actions that started in the past and continue or have present relevance ("She has studied for three hours.")
- **Past perfect:** an action completed before another past action ("She had already left when he arrived.")
- **Future:** actions that will happen ("She will take the test tomorrow.")

**Strategy:** Look at context clues (time words, other verbs) to determine the correct tense.

### Punctuation

**Commas:**
1. After introductory elements: "After the game, we went home."
2. Between items in a list: "apples, oranges, and bananas"
3. Before a coordinating conjunction joining independent clauses: "I studied, and I passed."
4. Around nonessential (parenthetical) information: "My dog, a golden retriever, loves swimming."
5. Do NOT use a comma between a subject and its verb or between a verb and its object

**Semicolons:**
- Join two related independent clauses: "I studied hard; I passed the test."
- Use before transitional words (however, therefore, moreover) when joining clauses: "I studied hard; however, I did not pass."

**Colons:**
- Introduce a list, explanation, or elaboration after an independent clause: "She brought three things: a book, a pen, and a notebook."
- The part before the colon must be a complete sentence

**Apostrophes:**
- Possession: "the dog's bone" (singular), "the dogs' bones" (plural)
- Contractions: "it's" = "it is"; "its" = possessive
- Do NOT use apostrophes for plurals: "The 1990s" not "The 1990's"

**Dashes:**
- Em dashes set off parenthetical information with emphasis: "The results — surprising to everyone — were published immediately."
- If you open with a dash, you must close with one (unless it ends the sentence)

### Modifiers

**A modifier must be placed next to the word it modifies.**

**Dangling modifier:**
- WRONG: "Running down the street, the rain started." (the rain was not running)
- RIGHT: "Running down the street, she got caught in the rain."

**Misplaced modifier:**
- WRONG: "She almost drove for 300 miles." (she almost drove? or she drove almost 300?)
- RIGHT: "She drove for almost 300 miles."

### Possessives vs. Plurals

- **Plural:** cats, boxes, children
- **Singular possessive:** cat's, box's, child's
- **Plural possessive:** cats', boxes', children's (irregular plurals add 's)

**Common confusions:**
- its (possessive) vs. it's (it is)
- their (possessive) vs. they're (they are) vs. there (location)
- whose (possessive) vs. who's (who is)
- your (possessive) vs. you're (you are)

## Domain 4: Expression of Ideas

### Transitions

**What it tests:** Choosing the most appropriate transition word or phrase to connect ideas.

**Types of transitions:**

| Relationship | Transitions |
|---|---|
| Addition | furthermore, moreover, in addition, also |
| Contrast | however, nevertheless, on the other hand, in contrast, yet |
| Cause/Effect | therefore, consequently, as a result, thus, because |
| Example | for example, for instance, specifically, in particular |
| Sequence | first, next, then, finally, subsequently |
| Comparison | similarly, likewise, in the same way |
| Concession | although, despite, even though, while, admittedly |
| Summary | in conclusion, overall, in summary, ultimately |

**Strategy:**
1. Read the sentence before and the sentence after the blank
2. Determine the logical relationship between the two ideas
3. Choose the transition that reflects that relationship
4. Read the completed passage to make sure it flows logically

**Example:**
"The study found that students who slept at least 8 hours performed better on exams. ______, the researchers recommended schools consider later start times."

The second sentence is a result of the first, so the answer is: **Therefore** or **Consequently**

### Rhetorical Synthesis

**What it tests:** Your ability to combine information from bullet points or notes into an effective sentence that accomplishes a specific goal.

**Question format:** You are given a set of notes (bullet points with information) and asked to write a sentence that effectively accomplishes a stated goal (e.g., "emphasize the difference between X and Y" or "introduce the topic to an unfamiliar audience").

**Strategy:**
1. Read the goal statement carefully — what specifically does the question want you to emphasize or accomplish?
2. Review the notes to identify which information is relevant to the goal
3. Eliminate answer choices that:
   - Do not address the stated goal
   - Include irrelevant information
   - Are grammatically flawed
   - Misrepresent the information in the notes
4. Choose the answer that most directly and effectively achieves the stated purpose

**Example notes:**
- The Hubble Space Telescope was launched in 1990
- It orbits Earth at an altitude of about 340 miles
- It has captured images of galaxies over 13 billion light-years away
- A servicing mission in 1993 corrected a flaw in its primary mirror

**Goal:** Emphasize the telescope's scientific achievements.

**Best answer:** "Since its launch in 1990, the Hubble Space Telescope has captured images of galaxies over 13 billion light-years away, making it one of the most productive scientific instruments in history."

(This focuses on achievements rather than technical specifications or repair history.)

## Passage Types You Will Encounter

### Literature
- Excerpts from fiction (novels, short stories)
- Focus on character, tone, narrative perspective
- Questions often ask about purpose, mood, or what a detail reveals about a character

### Social Science
- Topics from psychology, sociology, economics, political science
- Often present research findings, theories, or historical developments
- Questions may ask about main ideas, evidence, or implications of data

### Science
- Topics from biology, chemistry, physics, earth science
- May include descriptions of experiments, natural phenomena, or scientific debates
- Expect data in tables or charts accompanying some passages

### History/Humanities
- Historical documents, speeches, essays about art, philosophy, or culture
- May include excerpts from founding documents or global historical texts
- Questions often ask about purpose, rhetorical strategies, or point of view

### Poetry
- Short poems or excerpts
- Questions focus on meaning, imagery, tone, and structure
- Read carefully — every word matters in poetry

## General Test-Taking Strategies for Reading and Writing

### Before the Test
1. **Read widely.** Exposure to diverse texts (science articles, literature, historical documents) builds reading speed and comprehension naturally.
2. **Build vocabulary in context.** Do not memorize word lists in isolation. Learn words by reading and understanding them in sentences.
3. **Practice with official materials.** Use College Board practice tests and Khan Academy for the most accurate preparation.
4. **Time yourself.** Practice answering questions within the time limit (about 71 seconds per question).

### During the Test

1. **Read the question first, then the passage.** This focuses your reading and saves time. Know what you are looking for before you read.

2. **Read the entire passage.** Even though passages are short, every sentence can contain important information. Do not skim.

3. **Answer based on the text, not your opinion.** The correct answer is always supported by the passage. Even if you know something about the topic, only use information from the text.

4. **Use process of elimination.** On difficult questions, cross out answers you know are wrong. Often you can eliminate 2-3 choices and make an educated guess between the remaining options.

5. **Watch for extreme language.** Answers with words like "always," "never," "all," or "none" are often wrong. The SAT tends to favor moderate, carefully worded answers.

6. **Do not overthink.** If an answer seems clearly right, it probably is. The SAT is not trying to trick you — it is testing whether you can read carefully and think logically.

7. **Flag and move on.** If a question is taking too long, flag it and come back. Answer every question before time runs out.

8. **For grammar questions, trust your ear — then verify with rules.** If something sounds wrong, it probably is. But always confirm by identifying the specific grammar rule being tested.

9. **For transition questions, read the sentences on both sides of the blank.** The correct transition must logically connect both ideas.

10. **For vocabulary questions, substitute your choice into the passage.** Make sure the sentence still makes sense with your answer in place.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Choosing an answer just because it is true.** The answer must be supported by the specific passage, not by general knowledge.

2. **Falling for "half-right" answers.** Some choices start correctly but end with an inaccurate or unsupported claim. Read the entire answer choice.

3. **Misidentifying the main idea.** A detail mentioned in the passage is not the same as the main idea. The main idea encompasses the whole passage.

4. **Ignoring context for vocabulary.** The "obvious" meaning of a word may not be the meaning used in the passage.

5. **Making comma splices on convention questions.** Two independent clauses cannot be joined by just a comma.

6. **Choosing wordy answers.** On expression of ideas questions, the most concise answer that is grammatically correct and complete is usually best.

7. **Confusing correlation with causation.** Just because two things are associated does not mean one caused the other. Be precise about what the data shows.

---

*Consistent practice, careful reading, and knowing the question types will prepare you well for the SAT Reading and Writing section. Focus on understanding why correct answers are correct and why wrong answers are wrong.*
