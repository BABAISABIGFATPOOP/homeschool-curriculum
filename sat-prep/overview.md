# SAT Test Overview

## What Is the SAT?

The SAT (Scholastic Assessment Test) is a standardized college admissions test administered by the College Board. It is widely accepted by colleges and universities across the United States and internationally. The SAT measures your readiness for college by testing skills in reading, writing, and math that you develop over time through your coursework.

As of 2024, the SAT is administered digitally (the Digital SAT or DSAT). The test is adaptive, meaning the difficulty of the second module in each section adjusts based on your performance in the first module.

---

## Test Format

The Digital SAT has two main sections:

### 1. Reading and Writing (RW)
- **Time:** 64 minutes (two 32-minute modules)
- **Questions:** 54 total (27 per module)
- **Content:** Short passages (typically 25-150 words) each followed by a single question
- **Topics tested:**
  - Craft and Structure (words in context, text structure, cross-text connections)
  - Information and Ideas (central ideas, command of evidence, inferences)
  - Standard English Conventions (grammar, punctuation, sentence structure)
  - Expression of Ideas (rhetorical synthesis, transitions)

### 2. Math
- **Time:** 70 minutes (two 35-minute modules)
- **Questions:** 44 total (22 per module)
- **Calculator:** A built-in calculator (Desmos) is available for ALL math questions
- **Format:** Approximately 75% multiple choice (4 options) and 25% student-produced response (grid-in)
- **Topics tested:**
  - Algebra (linear equations, inequalities, systems)
  - Advanced Math (quadratics, polynomials, exponentials, radicals)
  - Problem Solving and Data Analysis (ratios, percentages, probability, statistics)
  - Geometry and Trigonometry (area, volume, triangles, circles, trig ratios)

### Total Test
- **Total Time:** 2 hours 14 minutes
- **Total Questions:** 98
- **Breaks:** There is a 10-minute break between the Reading/Writing section and the Math section

## Scoring

- **Total Score:** 400-1600
  - Reading and Writing: 200-800
  - Math: 200-800
- **No penalty for wrong answers** — always guess if you are unsure
- **Adaptive scoring:** Your score depends on both the number of correct answers and the difficulty level of your second module
- **Score Percentiles:**
  - 1600: 99th+ percentile
  - 1400: ~95th percentile
  - 1200: ~74th percentile
  - 1000: ~40th percentile
- **Average score:** Approximately 1050-1060 nationally

### Score Reporting
- Scores are typically available 2-3 weeks after the test date
- You can choose which scores to send to colleges (Score Choice)
- Some colleges practice "superscoring," taking your highest section scores across multiple test dates

## When to Take the SAT

### Recommended Timeline
- **Sophomore Year (10th grade):** Take the PSAT/NMSQT for practice
- **Junior Year (11th grade) — Fall/Winter:** Begin focused SAT prep
- **Junior Year — Spring:** Take the SAT for the first time (March or May)
- **Senior Year (12th grade) — Fall:** Retake if needed (August or October)

### Test Dates
The SAT is offered approximately 7 times per year, typically in:
- March
- May
- June
- August
- October
- November
- December

### Registration Deadlines
- Registration typically closes about 3-4 weeks before the test date
- Late registration (with a fee) closes about 2 weeks before the test date
- Check the College Board website for exact dates each year

## How to Register

1. **Create a College Board account** at [collegeboard.org](https://www.collegeboard.org)
2. **Log in** and select "Register for the SAT"
3. **Choose your test date** and preferred test center
4. **Upload a photo** (required for test day identification)
5. **Pay the fee:**
   - SAT: $60 (as of 2024-2025)
   - Fee waivers are available for students with financial need
6. **Print your admission ticket** before test day

### What to Bring on Test Day
- Admission ticket (printed or on your phone)
- Acceptable photo ID (school ID, driver's license, or passport)
- A fully charged device (laptop, tablet, or Chromebook) with the Bluebook app installed
- A charger (just in case)
- Pencils/pens for scratch work
- An approved calculator (optional, since Desmos is built in)
- Snacks and water for the break

## Top Study Tips

### General Strategies
1. **Start early** — Begin preparing at least 2-3 months before your test date
2. **Take a diagnostic test** — Identify your strengths and weaknesses before you study
3. **Focus on weaknesses** — Spend more time on the areas where you score lowest
4. **Practice with real materials** — Use official College Board practice tests available on Khan Academy and Bluebook
5. **Simulate test conditions** — Practice with timed, full-length tests to build stamina
6. **Review mistakes** — Understanding why you got a question wrong is more valuable than doing extra practice

### Reading and Writing Tips
- Read the question before the passage so you know what to look for
- Eliminate answers that are too extreme or not supported by the text
- For grammar questions, shorter and simpler answers are often correct
- Pay attention to transition words and logical connections

### Math Tips
- Read every question carefully — look for key words like "must," "could," or "cannot"
- Use the Desmos calculator strategically, especially for graphing
- Plug in answer choices (backsolving) when you are stuck
- Estimate when possible to eliminate wrong answers
- On grid-in questions, double-check your arithmetic

### Time Management
- Reading and Writing: About 1 minute 11 seconds per question
- Math: About 1 minute 35 seconds per question
- Flag difficult questions and come back to them
- Never leave a question blank — there is no guessing penalty

## SAT vs. ACT

| Feature | SAT | ACT |
|---|---|---|
| Sections | Reading/Writing, Math | English, Math, Reading, Science, (Writing optional) |
| Time | 2 hours 14 minutes | 2 hours 55 minutes (3:40 with writing) |
| Scoring | 400-1600 | 1-36 (composite) |
| Calculator | Allowed on all math | Allowed on all math |
| Science | No dedicated section | Yes |
| Format | Digital, adaptive | Paper or digital depending on state |
| Accepted by | Virtually all US colleges | Virtually all US colleges |

Most colleges accept both the SAT and ACT equally. Choose the test that best fits your strengths. If you are strong in science reasoning, the ACT may be a good fit. If you prefer a shorter, adaptive test, the SAT may be better.
