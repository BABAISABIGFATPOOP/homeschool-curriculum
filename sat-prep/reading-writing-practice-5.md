The simplest and most widely used voting system — plurality voting, in which each voter selects one candidate and the candidate with the most votes wins — suffers from a well-documented mathematical flaw known as the "spoiler effect." When three or more candidates compete, a candidate preferred by a majority can lose because a similar candidate splits the vote. This is not merely a theoretical concern; political scientists have documented numerous elections in which the winner received less than 40% of the vote while two similar candidates collectively received over 60%. Ranked-choice voting (RCV), which asks voters to rank candidates in order of preference, mitigates this problem through an iterative elimination process: the candidate with the fewest first-choice votes is eliminated, and those voters' second choices are redistributed until a candidate achieves a majority. Proponents argue that RCV reduces negative campaigning (since candidates benefit from being voters' second choice), produces winners with broader support, and gives voters more expressive power. Critics counter that RCV is more complex, harder for voters to understand, and can produce paradoxical results in which a candidate who would beat every other candidate in a head-to-head matchup can still lose under RCV — a violation of what mathematicians call the Condorcet criterion.
