# US History I CLEP Study Guide
## Colonization to 1877

## Exam Overview

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Format** | Multiple choice |
| **Number of Questions** | 120 |
| **Time Limit** | 90 minutes |
| **Passing Score** | ~50 out of 80 (scaled); approximately 50% correct |
| **College Credit** | Typically 3 semester hours |
| **Note** | Covers from pre-Columbian America through Reconstruction |

This exam tests knowledge of American history from before European contact through the end of Reconstruction (1877). Expect questions on political, economic, social, and cultural developments.

---

## Topics Covered

### 1. Pre-Columbian and Colonial America (10%)

#### Native Americans Before Contact
- Diverse civilizations: Pueblo (Southwest), Iroquois Confederacy (Northeast), Mississippian culture (mound builders), Plains Indians
- Agriculture, trade networks, varied social structures
- Adapted to different environments across the continent

#### European Exploration and Colonization
- **Spanish**: Columbus (1492), conquistadors (Cortes, Pizarro), encomienda system, mission system
- **French**: fur trade, alliances with Native Americans, exploration of Mississippi (La Salle)
- **English**: Jamestown (1607, first permanent English settlement), Plymouth (1620, Pilgrims), Massachusetts Bay (1630, Puritans)
- **Dutch**: New Amsterdam (later New York), Hudson River trade
- **Columbian Exchange**: transfer of plants, animals, diseases between Old World and New World; devastating impact of European diseases on Native populations

#### Colonial Regions
- **New England** (MA, CT, RI, NH): Puritan theocracy, subsistence farming, fishing, shipbuilding, town meetings
- **Middle Colonies** (NY, NJ, PA, DE): diverse populations, breadbasket, religious tolerance (especially PA)
- **Southern Colonies** (VA, MD, NC, SC, GA): plantation agriculture (tobacco, rice, indigo), slavery, Anglican church, gentry class

#### Colonial Society
- **Mercantilism**: colonies exist to benefit the mother country
- **Indentured servitude** transitioning to chattel slavery (especially after Bacon's Rebellion, 1676)
- **Salem Witch Trials** (1692): mass hysteria in Puritan Massachusetts
- **Great Awakening** (1730s-1740s): religious revival, Jonathan Edwards, George Whitefield; challenged established churches
- **Enlightenment influence**: reason, natural rights (Locke), self-governance

### 2. American Revolution (15%)

#### Causes
- **French and Indian War** (1754-1763): Britain wins but incurs massive debt
- **Proclamation of 1763**: banned settlement west of Appalachians
- **Taxation without representation**: Stamp Act (1765), Townshend Acts (1767), Tea Act (1773)
- **Colonial resistance**: Sons of Liberty, boycotts, Committees of Correspondence
- **Boston Massacre** (1770), **Boston Tea Party** (1773)
- **Intolerable Acts** (Coercive Acts, 1774): punishment for Boston; united colonial opposition
- **First Continental Congress** (1774): coordinated colonial response

#### The War
- **Lexington and Concord** (April 1775): "shot heard round the world"
- **Second Continental Congress** (1775): created Continental Army, appointed Washington as commander
- **Common Sense** (1776): Thomas Paine's pamphlet argued for independence
- **Declaration of Independence** (July 4, 1776): written by Jefferson; natural rights, social contract, list of grievances
- **Key battles**: Bunker Hill (1775), Saratoga (1777, turning point — secured French alliance), Yorktown (1781, decisive victory)
- **Treaty of Paris** (1783): Britain recognized American independence

#### Key Figures
- George Washington, Benjamin Franklin, Thomas Jefferson, John Adams, Alexander Hamilton, Thomas Paine, Marquis de Lafayette

### 3. Constitution and Early Republic (15%)

#### Articles of Confederation (1781-1789)
- First government: weak central government, no executive or judiciary
- Congress could not tax, regulate trade, or enforce laws
- **Shays' Rebellion** (1786-1787): highlighted weaknesses; pushed leaders toward a new constitution

#### Constitutional Convention (1787)
- **Virginia Plan**: bicameral legislature based on population (favored large states)
- **New Jersey Plan**: unicameral legislature with equal representation (favored small states)
- **Great Compromise**: bicameral legislature — Senate (equal) + House (proportional)
- **Three-Fifths Compromise**: enslaved persons counted as 3/5 for representation and taxation
- **Separation of powers**: legislative, executive, judicial branches
- **Checks and balances**: each branch can limit the others
- **Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists**: debate over ratification; Federalist Papers (Hamilton, Madison, Jay) argued for the Constitution
- **Bill of Rights** (1791): first 10 amendments, added to satisfy Anti-Federalists

#### Early Republic (1789-1824)
- **Washington's presidency**: established precedents (cabinet, two-term tradition, neutrality)
- **Hamilton vs. Jefferson**: national bank debate, loose vs. strict construction
- **Whiskey Rebellion** (1794): tested federal authority; Washington sent troops
- **John Adams**: Alien and Sedition Acts (1798), XYZ Affair
- **Jefferson**: Louisiana Purchase (1803), Lewis and Clark Expedition, Embargo Act (1807)
- **War of 1812**: "Second War of Independence," impressment, burning of Washington, Battle of New Orleans (Jackson), Treaty of Ghent
- **Monroe Doctrine** (1823): warned European nations against further colonization in the Americas
- **Era of Good Feelings**: period of one-party rule (Democratic-Republicans), nationalism

### 4. Jacksonian Era (10%)

- **Election of 1824**: "corrupt bargain" — Adams won despite Jackson's plurality; Henry Clay's role
- **Election of 1828**: Jackson wins; rise of the "common man," expansion of suffrage (white males)
- **Jacksonian Democracy**: populism, spoils system, opposition to elites
- **Indian Removal Act** (1830): forced relocation of Native Americans; **Trail of Tears** (Cherokee, 1838)
- **Nullification Crisis** (1832-1833): South Carolina tried to nullify federal tariffs; Jackson threatened force
- **Bank War**: Jackson vetoed recharter of Second Bank of the United States
- **Whig Party**: formed in opposition to Jackson (Henry Clay, Daniel Webster)
- **Manifest Destiny**: belief that American expansion westward was inevitable and divinely ordained

### 5. Antebellum Period (10%)

#### Slavery and Abolition
- **Cotton gin** (1793, Eli Whitney): increased demand for slave labor
- **Plantation system**: cash crops (cotton, tobacco, sugar), slave codes, resistance (revolts, Underground Railroad)
- **Abolitionist movement**: Frederick Douglass, William Lloyd Garrison (The Liberator), Harriet Tubman, Harriet Beecher Stowe (Uncle Tom's Cabin)
- **Nat Turner's Rebellion** (1831): slave revolt in Virginia; led to harsher slave codes

#### Sectional Tensions
- **Missouri Compromise** (1820): Missouri = slave, Maine = free, 36°30' line
- **Compromise of 1850**: California = free, Fugitive Slave Act strengthened, popular sovereignty in Utah/New Mexico
- **Kansas-Nebraska Act** (1854): popular sovereignty; led to "Bleeding Kansas"
- **Dred Scott v. Sandford** (1857): Supreme Court ruled enslaved people were not citizens, Congress could not ban slavery in territories
- **Lincoln-Douglas debates** (1858): slavery expansion; elevated Lincoln's national profile
- **John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry** (1859): attempted slave insurrection

#### Reform Movements
- **Second Great Awakening**: religious revival driving social reform
- **Temperance movement**: opposition to alcohol
- **Women's rights**: Seneca Falls Convention (1848), Declaration of Sentiments (Elizabeth Cady Stanton, Lucretia Mott)
- **Education reform**: Horace Mann, public schooling
- **Transcendentalism**: Emerson, Thoreau; individualism, nature, self-reliance

### 6. Westward Expansion (5%)

- **Oregon Trail**: thousands of settlers moved west in the 1840s
- **Texas annexation** (1845): independent republic since 1836 (Battle of the Alamo)
- **Mexican-American War** (1846-1848): Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo; U.S. gained California, New Mexico, Arizona, etc.
- **California Gold Rush** (1849): massive migration, rapid statehood
- **Homestead Act** (1862): free land to settlers who improved it
- **Transcontinental Railroad** (completed 1869): connected East and West

### 7. Civil War (15%)

#### Causes
- Slavery (fundamental cause), states' rights, economic differences (industrial North vs. agricultural South), political breakdowns
- **Election of 1860**: Lincoln wins without Southern support; Southern states secede
- **Confederate States of America** formed (Jefferson Davis, president)
- **Fort Sumter** (April 1861): first shots of the war

#### Key Events
- **Bull Run / Manassas** (1861): first major battle; showed the war would be long
- **Antietam** (1862): bloodiest single day; Union "victory" allowed Lincoln to issue Emancipation Proclamation
- **Emancipation Proclamation** (Jan 1, 1863): freed enslaved people in Confederate states; changed the war's purpose
- **Gettysburg** (July 1863): turning point in the East; Lee's last major offensive failed
- **Vicksburg** (July 1863): Union control of Mississippi River, split the Confederacy
- **Sherman's March to the Sea** (1864): total war through Georgia
- **Appomattox Court House** (April 9, 1865): Lee surrendered to Grant

#### Key Figures
- **Union**: Lincoln, Grant, Sherman, Frederick Douglass
- **Confederacy**: Jefferson Davis, Robert E. Lee, Stonewall Jackson

#### Home Front
- **Draft riots** (especially NYC, 1863)
- Women's expanded roles (nursing, factory work)
- Economic impacts: wartime industrialization in North, devastation in South
- **13th Amendment** (1865): abolished slavery

### 8. Reconstruction (10%)

#### Presidential Reconstruction (1865-1867)
- **Lincoln's plan**: lenient, "10% Plan" — 10% of voters take loyalty oath, state readmitted
- **Lincoln assassinated** (April 14, 1865) by John Wilkes Booth
- **Andrew Johnson**: continued lenient approach, vetoed civil rights legislation, impeached (but acquitted)
- **Black Codes**: Southern laws restricting freedmen's rights, resembled slavery

#### Congressional (Radical) Reconstruction (1867-1877)
- **Radical Republicans**: Thaddeus Stevens, Charles Sumner; wanted to punish South and protect freedmen
- **Reconstruction Acts** (1867): divided South into military districts, required new constitutions with Black male suffrage
- **14th Amendment** (1868): citizenship and equal protection for all persons born in the U.S.
- **15th Amendment** (1870): voting rights cannot be denied based on race
- **Freedmen's Bureau**: assisted formerly enslaved people with education, food, legal aid
- **Carpetbaggers and scalawags**: Northerners who moved South, Southern whites who supported Reconstruction

#### End of Reconstruction
- **Ku Klux Klan**: white supremacist terrorism against Black citizens and Republicans
- **Compromise of 1877**: Hayes becomes president; federal troops withdrawn from the South
- **Jim Crow era begins**: segregation, disenfranchisement, sharecropping system

---

## Study Tips

1. Create a timeline of major events — chronology questions are common.
2. Know the causes and effects of major conflicts (Revolution, Civil War).
3. Understand the different compromises over slavery and why each failed to prevent war.
4. Focus on key documents: Declaration of Independence, Constitution, Emancipation Proclamation.
5. Know the key amendments: 1st-10th (Bill of Rights), 13th, 14th, 15th.
6. Understand the different perspectives: North vs. South, Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists, etc.

## Practice Questions

**1.** The Great Compromise at the Constitutional Convention resolved the dispute over:

A) Slavery
B) Presidential power
C) Legislative representation
D) Taxation

**2.** Which event is considered the turning point of the American Revolution because it secured a French alliance?

A) Battle of Yorktown
B) Battle of Bunker Hill
C) Battle of Saratoga
D) Crossing the Delaware

**3.** The Monroe Doctrine (1823) was primarily aimed at:

A) Establishing trade relations with China
B) Preventing European colonization in the Americas
C) Expanding slavery into new territories
D) Creating an alliance with Great Britain

**4.** The Trail of Tears refers to:

A) The march of the Continental Army to Valley Forge
B) The forced relocation of Native Americans to Indian Territory
C) Sherman's March to the Sea during the Civil War
D) The westward journey along the Oregon Trail

**5.** Which compromise admitted California as a free state and included a stronger Fugitive Slave Act?

A) Missouri Compromise
B) Compromise of 1850
C) Kansas-Nebraska Act
D) Three-Fifths Compromise

**6.** The Emancipation Proclamation (1863) freed enslaved people in:

A) All states, both Union and Confederate
B) Only the border states
C) Only the Confederate states in rebellion
D) Only Washington, D.C.

**7.** Shays' Rebellion (1786-1787) demonstrated the weakness of the:

A) Constitution
B) Articles of Confederation
C) Bill of Rights
D) Monroe Doctrine

**8.** The 14th Amendment (1868) established:

A) The abolition of slavery
B) Women's right to vote
C) Citizenship and equal protection under the law
D) Prohibition of alcohol

**9.** Which was NOT a cause of the War of 1812?

A) British impressment of American sailors
B) British support of Native American resistance
C) Desire to annex Canada
D) Disputes over the Louisiana Purchase

**10.** The Compromise of 1877 effectively ended Reconstruction by:

A) Passing the 15th Amendment
B) Removing federal troops from the South
C) Electing Andrew Johnson as president
D) Establishing the Freedmen's Bureau

---

## Answer Key

1. **C) Legislative representation** — The Great Compromise created a bicameral legislature with the Senate (equal representation) and House (proportional representation).
2. **C) Battle of Saratoga** — The American victory at Saratoga (1777) convinced France to enter the war as an American ally.
3. **B) Preventing European colonization in the Americas** — Monroe warned European powers not to establish new colonies or interfere in the Western Hemisphere.
4. **B) The forced relocation of Native Americans to Indian Territory** — Primarily the Cherokee in 1838, under the Indian Removal Act. Thousands died on the journey.
5. **B) Compromise of 1850** — This package deal attempted to balance free and slave state interests and included the controversial Fugitive Slave Act.
6. **C) Only the Confederate states in rebellion** — Lincoln strategically freed enslaved people only in rebelling states; border states and Union-occupied areas were excluded.
7. **B) Articles of Confederation** — The central government's inability to raise an army or respond to the rebellion showed the Articles were too weak.
8. **C) Citizenship and equal protection under the law** — The 14th Amendment granted citizenship to all persons born in the U.S. and guaranteed equal protection (13th abolished slavery, 19th gave women the vote).
9. **D) Disputes over the Louisiana Purchase** — The Louisiana Purchase (1803) was not a cause. The war was driven by impressment, trade restrictions, and British-Native American alliances.
10. **B) Removing federal troops from the South** — In exchange for accepting Hayes as president, Democrats got federal troops removed, ending Reconstruction and paving the way for Jim Crow.
