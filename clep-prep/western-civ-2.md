# Western Civilization II CLEP Study Guide
## 1648 to Present

## Exam Overview

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Format** | Multiple choice |
| **Number of Questions** | 120 |
| **Time Limit** | 90 minutes |

This exam covers the political, economic, social, cultural, and intellectual history of Western civilization from the mid-17th century to the present.

---

## Topics Covered

### 1. Absolutism and Constitutionalism (10%)

#### Absolutism
- **Theory**: divine right of kings — monarch's authority comes from God, not the people
- **Louis XIV of France** (1643-1715): "I am the state," Palace of Versailles, centralized power, revoked Edict of Nantes (1685), wars of expansion
- **Peter the Great of Russia** (1682-1725): westernization, built St. Petersburg, modernized military and government
- **Frederick the Great of Prussia**: enlightened absolutism, military power, religious tolerance
- **Habsburg Empire**: Austria; multi-ethnic, Catholic; Maria Theresa, Joseph II (enlightened reforms)

#### Constitutionalism
- **English Civil War** (1642-1649): Parliament vs. King Charles I; Puritans (Cromwell) won; Charles executed
- **Oliver Cromwell**: Lord Protector, Puritan republic; ultimately unpopular
- **Glorious Revolution** (1688): William and Mary replaced James II; bloodless; Parliament supreme
- **English Bill of Rights** (1689): limited royal power, guaranteed Parliamentary rights, no standing army without consent
- **John Locke**: natural rights (life, liberty, property), social contract, consent of the governed; hugely influential on Enlightenment and American Revolution
- **Dutch Republic**: commercial power, religious tolerance, decentralized government

**Key Concepts:**
- Absolutism = centralized power in the monarch; constitutionalism = power limited by law/legislature
- England moved toward constitutional monarchy while France became the model of absolutism

### 2. Enlightenment (10%)

#### Key Ideas
- **Reason**: human reason can understand and improve the world
- **Natural law**: universal laws govern human society (like Newton's laws govern nature)
- **Progress**: society can improve through reason, science, and education
- **Religious tolerance**: skepticism of organized religion; Deism (God as watchmaker)
- **Individual rights**: liberty, equality, property

#### Major Thinkers
- **John Locke** (1632-1704): natural rights, social contract, tabula rasa (blank slate), Two Treatises of Government
- **Voltaire** (1694-1778): freedom of speech and religion, attacked the Catholic Church, wit and satire
- **Montesquieu** (1689-1755): separation of powers (executive, legislative, judicial); The Spirit of the Laws
- **Jean-Jacques Rousseau** (1712-1778): The Social Contract, general will, "man is born free but everywhere is in chains," noble savage; influenced Romanticism
- **Adam Smith** (1723-1790): The Wealth of Nations, free market economics, invisible hand, laissez-faire; foundation of capitalism
- **Denis Diderot**: Encyclopedie — compiled Enlightenment knowledge
- **Mary Wollstonecraft**: A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792); argued for women's education and equality
- **Cesare Beccaria**: On Crimes and Punishments; opposed torture and capital punishment

#### Enlightened Despots
- **Frederick the Great** (Prussia): religious tolerance, legal reform, promoted arts and philosophy
- **Catherine the Great** (Russia): corresponded with philosophes, but maintained serfdom
- **Joseph II** (Austria): abolished serfdom, religious tolerance; many reforms reversed after his death

#### Scientific Revolution (Context for Enlightenment)
- **Copernicus**: heliocentric theory (1543)
- **Galileo**: telescope observations, confirmed heliocentrism; conflict with the Church
- **Kepler**: laws of planetary motion
- **Newton**: laws of motion, gravity, calculus; Principia Mathematica (1687)
- **Scientific method**: observation, hypothesis, experimentation, conclusion

### 3. French Revolution and Napoleon (12%)

#### Causes
- **Social inequality**: Three Estates — First Estate (clergy), Second Estate (nobility), Third Estate (everyone else, 97%)
- **Financial crisis**: debt from wars (including American Revolution), extravagant spending, poor harvests
- **Enlightenment ideas**: liberty, equality, popular sovereignty
- **Weak leadership**: Louis XVI indecisive; Marie Antoinette unpopular

#### Revolution (1789-1799)
- **Estates-General** (May 1789): Third Estate broke away, formed National Assembly
- **Tennis Court Oath** (June 1789): vowed to write a constitution
- **Storming of the Bastille** (July 14, 1789): symbolic start of the Revolution
- **Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen** (August 1789): liberty, property, security, resistance to oppression; influenced by Enlightenment
- **Women's March on Versailles** (October 1789): forced royal family to Paris
- **Civil Constitution of the Clergy** (1790): subordinated Church to state
- **Legislative Assembly** (1791-1792): constitutional monarchy, war with Austria and Prussia
- **National Convention** (1792-1795): republic declared, Louis XVI executed (January 1793)
- **Reign of Terror** (1793-1794): Robespierre and Committee of Public Safety; ~17,000 executed by guillotine; ended when Robespierre himself was executed (Thermidorian Reaction)
- **Directory** (1795-1799): weak, corrupt government

#### Napoleon Bonaparte (1799-1815)
- **Coup of 18 Brumaire** (1799): seized power; became First Consul, then Emperor (1804)
- **Napoleonic Code**: unified legal system; equality before the law, property rights, secular state; BUT reduced women's rights
- **Military conquests**: dominated most of continental Europe by 1810
- **Continental System**: economic warfare against Britain (blockade); largely failed
- **Invasion of Russia** (1812): disastrous retreat from Moscow; beginning of the end
- **Exile to Elba** (1814), escaped, **Hundred Days**, defeated at **Waterloo** (June 1815), exiled to St. Helena
- **Congress of Vienna** (1814-1815): Metternich; restored old order; balance of power; Concert of Europe; tried to suppress liberal/nationalist movements

### 4. Industrial Revolution (10%)

#### Origins (Britain, late 1700s)
- **Why Britain?**: natural resources (coal, iron), stable government, colonial markets, agricultural revolution, capital for investment, entrepreneurial culture
- **Agricultural Revolution**: enclosure movement, crop rotation, new tools (seed drill); displaced workers moved to cities

#### Key Developments
- **Textile industry**: spinning jenny (Hargreaves), water frame (Arkwright), spinning mule (Crompton), power loom
- **Steam engine**: James Watt; powered factories, mines, transportation
- **Iron and steel**: Bessemer process (cheap steel)
- **Transportation**: railroads (Stephenson's Rocket), steamships, canals
- **Factory system**: centralized production, division of labor, mechanization

#### Social Effects
- **Urbanization**: rapid city growth; overcrowding, pollution, disease
- **Working conditions**: long hours (12-16 hrs), low wages, child labor, unsafe factories
- **Class structure**: new industrial middle class (bourgeoisie) and working class (proletariat)
- **Women and children**: exploited as cheap labor

#### Responses
- **Luddites**: workers who destroyed machines
- **Labor unions**: organized for better conditions; initially illegal
- **Reform legislation**: Factory Acts (limited child labor, working hours)
- **Socialism**: collective ownership of production; response to inequality
- **Karl Marx**: The Communist Manifesto (1848, with Engels), Das Kapital; class struggle, proletariat revolution, dictatorship of the proletariat, classless society
- **Utopian socialists**: Robert Owen, Charles Fourier; ideal communities

### 5. Nationalism and Nation-Building (10%)

#### Ideologies After 1815
- **Conservatism**: preserve tradition, monarchy, established religion; Metternich
- **Liberalism**: individual rights, constitutionalism, free markets, limited government
- **Nationalism**: loyalty to one's nation/ethnic group; desire for self-determination
- **Romanticism**: emotion over reason, nature, folk traditions, individual expression; reaction against Enlightenment rationalism

#### Revolutions of 1848
- "Springtime of the Peoples" — wave of revolutions across Europe (France, German states, Austrian Empire, Italy)
- Causes: economic hardship, desire for liberal reforms and national unification
- Most failed in the short term but spread nationalist ideas

#### Italian Unification (1859-1870)
- **Mazzini**: nationalist ideals, Young Italy
- **Cavour**: Prime Minister of Piedmont-Sardinia; realpolitik, allied with France
- **Garibaldi**: conquered southern Italy with his Red Shirts
- Unified under the Kingdom of Piedmont-Sardinia; Rome added 1870

#### German Unification (1864-1871)
- **Otto von Bismarck**: "Iron Chancellor" of Prussia; realpolitik, "blood and iron"
- Three wars: Danish War (1864), Austro-Prussian War (1866), Franco-Prussian War (1870-1871)
- **German Empire** proclaimed at Versailles (January 1871); Kaiser Wilhelm I
- Bismarck's domestic policies: Kulturkampf (vs. Catholic Church), anti-socialist laws, welfare state

#### Other Nationalism
- **Austro-Hungarian Compromise** (1867): dual monarchy
- **Ottoman decline**: "sick man of Europe," Balkan nationalism
- **Irish nationalism**: resistance to British rule

### 6. Imperialism (7%)

- **New Imperialism** (1870s-1914): European powers carved up Africa and Asia
- **Motivations**: economic (raw materials, markets), strategic, nationalist prestige, Social Darwinism, "White Man's Burden" (Kipling), missionary zeal
- **Scramble for Africa**: Berlin Conference (1884-1885) divided Africa among European powers; no African representation
- **British Empire**: India ("jewel of the crown"), Egypt, South Africa (Boer War)
- **French Empire**: North and West Africa, Indochina
- **Belgian Congo**: brutal exploitation under Leopold II
- **Opium Wars**: Britain vs. China; unequal treaties; spheres of influence
- **Japan**: Meiji Restoration (1868); rapid industrialization, became an imperial power

### 7. World War I (10%)

#### Causes (MAIN)
- **Militarism**: arms race, especially Germany vs. Britain (naval)
- **Alliances**: Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) vs. Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain)
- **Imperialism**: colonial rivalries
- **Nationalism**: especially in the Balkans ("powder keg of Europe")
- **Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand** (June 28, 1914) by Gavrilo Princip in Sarajevo

#### The War (1914-1918)
- **Western Front**: trench warfare in France/Belgium; stalemate; Verdun, the Somme (horrific casualties)
- **Eastern Front**: more mobile but equally devastating; Russia struggled
- **New technology**: machine guns, poison gas, tanks, airplanes, submarines (U-boats)
- **Total war**: entire economies mobilized; civilian populations affected
- **U.S. entry** (1917): tipped the balance toward the Allies
- **Russian Revolution** (1917): Russia exited the war (Treaty of Brest-Litovsk)
- **Armistice**: November 11, 1918

#### Aftermath
- **Treaty of Versailles** (1919): war guilt clause (Article 231) on Germany, reparations, territorial losses, military restrictions
- **League of Nations**: Wilson's idea; U.S. did not join
- **Collapse of empires**: Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, German
- **Mandate system**: former colonies redistributed, not independent
- **"Lost Generation"**: disillusionment in art and literature

### 8. World War II (10%)

#### Interwar Period
- **Russian Revolution** (1917): Bolsheviks (Lenin); civil war; USSR established; Stalin's rule — Five-Year Plans, collectivization, Great Purge
- **Fascism**: extreme nationalism, authoritarian, anti-communist
  - **Italy**: Mussolini (1922), fascist state
  - **Germany**: Hitler, Nazi Party, Mein Kampf; came to power 1933; Nuremberg Laws (anti-Jewish), remilitarization, expansionism
- **Great Depression**: global economic collapse; fueled extremism
- **Appeasement**: Munich Agreement (1938); Chamberlain gave Hitler the Sudetenland; "peace in our time"

#### The War (1939-1945)
- **German invasion of Poland** (September 1, 1939): war began
- **Blitzkrieg**: rapid warfare; France fell in 6 weeks (1940)
- **Battle of Britain** (1940): RAF defended against German air attacks; Britain held
- **Operation Barbarossa** (1941): Germany invaded the USSR; eventually stalled
- **Stalingrad** (1942-1943): turning point on Eastern Front; devastating German defeat
- **D-Day** (June 6, 1944): Allied invasion of Normandy; liberation of Western Europe
- **V-E Day**: May 8, 1945
- **Pacific**: Pearl Harbor (1941), island hopping, atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki (August 1945); V-J Day

#### Holocaust
- **Systematic genocide**: 6 million Jews murdered, plus millions of Roma, disabled, political opponents, homosexuals, and others
- **Concentration and death camps**: Auschwitz, Treblinka, Dachau
- **Nuremberg Trials**: Nazi leaders tried for war crimes and crimes against humanity

### 9. Cold War (10%)

- **Iron Curtain**: Churchill's speech (1946); division of Europe
- **Containment**: U.S. policy to prevent spread of communism (Truman Doctrine, Marshall Plan)
- **NATO** (1949) vs. **Warsaw Pact** (1955)
- **Berlin Blockade/Airlift** (1948-1949), **Berlin Wall** (1961-1989)
- **Korean War** (1950-1953): containment in Asia
- **Hungarian Uprising** (1956): crushed by Soviets
- **Cuban Missile Crisis** (1962): closest to nuclear war
- **Prague Spring** (1968): Czechoslovakia; crushed by Soviets
- **Detente** (1970s): easing tensions, arms control (SALT, Helsinki Accords)
- **Solidarity** (Poland, 1980s): Lech Walesa; labor movement against communist rule
- **Gorbachev**: glasnost (openness), perestroika (restructuring); unintended consequences
- **Fall of the Berlin Wall** (November 9, 1989)
- **Collapse of the Soviet Union** (1991): Cold War ends

#### Decolonization
- **Post-WWII independence movements**: India (1947, Gandhi/Nehru), African nations (1950s-1960s), Vietnam, Algeria
- **Non-Aligned Movement**: countries seeking independence from both superpowers

### 10. Modern Europe (6%)

- **European integration**: European Coal and Steel Community (1951), EEC/Common Market (1957), European Union (Maastricht Treaty, 1992), Euro (1999)
- **Welfare states**: expanded social programs in Western Europe (healthcare, education, pensions)
- **Thatcher** (UK) and **Reagan** (US): conservative reaction; free market economics, reduced welfare
- **Reunification of Germany** (1990)
- **Yugoslav Wars** (1990s): ethnic conflict, genocide in Bosnia (Srebrenica); Kosovo
- **Terrorism**: IRA in Britain, ETA in Spain; September 11 and global War on Terror
- **Immigration debates**: multiculturalism, integration, rising nationalism
- **2008 Financial Crisis**: global recession, austerity measures, Eurozone crisis
- **Brexit** (2016 vote, 2020 departure): UK left the European Union

---

## Study Tips

1. The French Revolution and both World Wars make up the largest portions — study them thoroughly.
2. Know the major "-isms": absolutism, liberalism, nationalism, imperialism, socialism, fascism, communism.
3. Understand cause-and-effect chains (e.g., WWI → Treaty of Versailles → rise of Hitler → WWII).
4. Be able to match Enlightenment thinkers with their key ideas.
5. Know the key dates and turning points for each major conflict.
6. Understand the Cold War as a global ideological struggle, not just a series of events.

## Practice Questions

**1.** Which Enlightenment thinker argued for the separation of government into executive, legislative, and judicial branches?

A) Voltaire
B) Rousseau
C) Montesquieu
D) Locke

**2.** The storming of the Bastille (July 14, 1789) symbolized:

A) Napoleon's rise to power
B) The beginning of the French Revolution
C) The end of the Reign of Terror
D) The Congress of Vienna

**3.** Which of the following was a major result of the Congress of Vienna (1815)?

A) The unification of Germany
B) The restoration of conservative monarchies and a balance of power in Europe
C) The creation of the League of Nations
D) The spread of democratic government across Europe

**4.** Karl Marx argued that history is driven by:

A) Great individuals
B) Religious conflict
C) Class struggle between the bourgeoisie and the proletariat
D) Technological innovation

**5.** The "scramble for Africa" was formalized at the:

A) Congress of Vienna (1815)
B) Treaty of Versailles (1919)
C) Berlin Conference (1884-1885)
D) Munich Conference (1938)

**6.** Which of the following was NOT a cause of World War I?

A) The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand
B) The invasion of Poland
C) Alliance systems
D) Militarism and the arms race

**7.** The Treaty of Versailles (1919) placed primary blame for World War I on:

A) Austria-Hungary
B) Russia
C) Germany
D) The Ottoman Empire

**8.** The policy of appeasement before World War II is most associated with:

A) Winston Churchill
B) Neville Chamberlain
C) Franklin Roosevelt
D) Joseph Stalin

**9.** The fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 symbolized:

A) The beginning of the Cold War
B) The end of World War II
C) The collapse of communism in Eastern Europe
D) German unification under Bismarck

**10.** Which Soviet leader introduced the policies of glasnost and perestroika?

A) Stalin
B) Khrushchev
C) Brezhnev
D) Gorbachev

---

## Answer Key

1. **C) Montesquieu** — In The Spirit of the Laws, Montesquieu argued that political power should be divided into three branches to prevent tyranny.
2. **B) The beginning of the French Revolution** — The Bastille was a prison symbolizing royal authority; its fall marked the people's uprising against the old regime.
3. **B) The restoration of conservative monarchies and a balance of power** — Led by Metternich, the Congress sought to undo the changes of the French Revolution and Napoleon and restore stability.
4. **C) Class struggle between the bourgeoisie and the proletariat** — Marx's theory of historical materialism holds that economic class conflict drives historical change.
5. **C) Berlin Conference (1884-1885)** — European powers met to divide Africa among themselves, with no African representatives present.
6. **B) The invasion of Poland** — The invasion of Poland in 1939 started World War II, not World War I.
7. **C) Germany** — Article 231 (the "war guilt clause") placed sole responsibility for the war on Germany and its allies.
8. **B) Neville Chamberlain** — British PM Chamberlain agreed to let Hitler annex the Sudetenland at Munich (1938), believing it would prevent war.
9. **C) The collapse of communism in Eastern Europe** — The Berlin Wall's fall on November 9, 1989 was the most powerful symbol of communism's collapse in Europe.
10. **D) Gorbachev** — Mikhail Gorbachev introduced glasnost (openness/transparency) and perestroika (economic restructuring) in the 1980s, which inadvertently led to the Soviet Union's dissolution.
