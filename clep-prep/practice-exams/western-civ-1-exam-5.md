# Western Civilization I CLEP Practice Exam 5

**Time Limit:** 90 minutes
**Number of Questions:** 60
**Passing Score:** Approximately 50 (scaled score of 50 out of 80)

**Instructions:** Choose the best answer (A, B, C, or D) for each question. This exam covers Western civilization from ancient times through the early modern period (approximately 1648).

---

**1.** The earliest known civilization in Mesopotamia, located between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, was:

A) The Egyptian civilization
B) The Sumerian civilization
C) The Greek civilization
D) The Roman civilization

**2.** The Code of Ur-Nammu, predating Hammurabi's Code, is significant as:

A) The first known military treaty
B) One of the oldest surviving legal codes
C) A religious text
D) An astronomical guide

**3.** Egyptian hieroglyphics were deciphered in the 19th century thanks to:

A) A translation found in a Roman library
B) The Rosetta Stone, which had the same text in three scripts
C) An Egyptian scholar who survived into modern times
D) A Greek manuscript discovered in Athens

**4.** The Hittites were among the first civilizations to:

A) Develop democracy
B) Smelt and use iron on a large scale
C) Build pyramids
D) Invent the alphabet

**5.** The Persian Empire under Cyrus the Great was notable for its:

A) Extreme cruelty toward conquered peoples
B) Relatively tolerant treatment of conquered peoples and their religions
C) Small geographical size
D) Rejection of all trade with neighbors

**6.** The Spartans' military-focused society included:

A) Universal education for all citizens in philosophy
B) Rigorous training for male citizens beginning in childhood (the agoge)
C) A ban on all forms of combat
D) A democratic system identical to Athens

**7.** The Peloponnesian War (431-404 BCE) was fought primarily between:

A) Greece and Persia
B) Athens and Sparta
C) Rome and Carthage
D) Egypt and Mesopotamia

**8.** Herodotus is often called the "Father of History" because he:

A) Invented writing
B) Was among the first to systematically investigate and record historical events
C) Led the Greek army against Persia
D) Founded the city of Athens

**9.** The Parthenon in Athens was dedicated to:

A) Zeus
B) Athena
C) Apollo
D) Poseidon

**10.** Greek tragedy, as exemplified by playwrights such as Sophocles and Euripides, typically explored:

A) Comedy and farce
B) Human suffering, moral dilemmas, and the relationship between humans and the divine
C) Agricultural techniques
D) Military strategy

**11.** The conquests of Alexander the Great extended Greek influence as far east as:

A) Italy
B) India
C) China
D) Sub-Saharan Africa

**12.** The Library of Alexandria was significant because it:

A) Was the first building made of stone
B) Represented an effort to collect all known knowledge in the ancient world
C) Was a military fortress
D) Housed only religious texts

**13.** The Roman Republic replaced the earlier Roman monarchy in approximately:

A) 753 BCE
B) 509 BCE
C) 44 BCE
D) 27 BCE

**14.** The Twelve Tables of Roman law were important because they:

A) Established a monarchy
B) Were the first written laws of Rome, making legal rules publicly known
C) Abolished all forms of government
D) Applied only to slaves

**15.** The Gracchi brothers (Tiberius and Gaius) are remembered for:

A) Conquering Gaul
B) Attempting land and social reforms to address inequality in the late Roman Republic
C) Founding Christianity
D) Building the Colosseum

**16.** The transition from the Roman Republic to the Roman Empire is often dated to:

A) The founding of Rome
B) Augustus becoming the first emperor in 27 BCE
C) The fall of Carthage
D) The death of Alexander the Great

**17.** Roman engineering achievements include all of the following EXCEPT:

A) Aqueducts
B) Concrete construction
C) The printing press
D) A vast road network

**18.** The apostle Paul was significant in the spread of Christianity because he:

A) Was the first pope
B) Carried the message of Christianity to non-Jewish (Gentile) communities throughout the Roman world
C) Was a Roman emperor who converted
D) Wrote the entire New Testament alone

**19.** The Edict of Thessalonica (380 CE) made Christianity:

A) Illegal throughout the empire
B) The official state religion of the Roman Empire
C) Optional for Roman citizens
D) A philosophical school rather than a religion

**20.** The Germanic migrations and invasions that contributed to Rome's fall included groups such as:

A) The Persians and Egyptians
B) The Visigoths, Vandals, and Ostrogoths
C) The Greeks and Phoenicians
D) The Chinese and Indians

**21.** The role of monasteries in the early Middle Ages included:

A) Only military training
B) Preserving learning, copying manuscripts, and providing education
C) Governing kingdoms
D) Conducting trade exclusively

**22.** The Carolingian Renaissance under Charlemagne involved:

A) The conquest of the Byzantine Empire
B) A revival of learning, art, and scholarship in Western Europe
C) The invention of gunpowder
D) The founding of the first university

**23.** The Vikings are historically significant for their:

A) Peaceful farming communities that never left Scandinavia
B) Maritime exploration, trade, and raids across Europe, and settlement in places from Iceland to Russia
C) Invention of the compass
D) Founding of the Roman Empire

**24.** The feudal relationship between lord and vassal was formalized through:

A) A democratic election
B) An oath of loyalty and the granting of a fief (land)
C) A monetary payment
D) A religious ceremony conducted by the pope

**25.** The role of the medieval Catholic Church in European life included:

A) Only spiritual matters with no political influence
B) Significant spiritual, political, educational, and cultural authority
C) Complete separation from the state
D) Only collecting taxes

**26.** The Great Schism of 1054 divided Christianity into:

A) Protestantism and Catholicism
B) The Roman Catholic Church in the West and the Eastern Orthodox Church
C) Christianity and Islam
D) Anglicanism and Lutheranism

**27.** The Dominican and Franciscan orders, founded in the 13th century, were significant for:

A) Their military campaigns
B) Their emphasis on preaching, poverty, and education
C) Their rejection of Christianity
D) Their wealth and political power

**28.** The Reconquista was the centuries-long effort to:

A) Conquer the Byzantine Empire
B) Reclaim the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rule
C) Convert all of Asia to Christianity
D) Explore the New World

**29.** Scholasticism was a medieval intellectual tradition that sought to:

A) Reject all ancient knowledge
B) Reconcile faith and reason through systematic philosophical inquiry
C) Replace Christianity with Greek religion
D) Promote only scientific study

**30.** The Hanseatic League was:

A) A military alliance of kingdoms
B) A commercial confederation of merchant guilds and market towns in northern Europe
C) A religious order
D) A league of medieval universities

**31.** The Great Famine of 1315-1317 in Europe was caused primarily by:

A) Warfare destroying all farms
B) Severe weather and crop failures
C) A volcanic eruption
D) An outbreak of plague

**32.** The Avignon Papacy (1309-1377) weakened papal authority because:

A) The pope was captured by Muslims
B) The popes resided in Avignon under French influence rather than in Rome
C) The papacy was dissolved
D) Two popes ruled simultaneously from Rome

**33.** The Western Schism (1378-1417) involved:

A) A split between Christianity and Islam
B) Rival claimants to the papacy, with two and later three popes ruling simultaneously
C) The founding of Protestantism
D) A war between England and France

**34.** Geoffrey Chaucer's *The Canterbury Tales* is significant in literary history for:

A) Being written in Latin
B) Being one of the first major works of English literature written in the vernacular
C) Describing only religious subjects
D) Founding the Renaissance in Italy

**35.** The significance of Petrarch in Renaissance history is that he:

A) Invented the printing press
B) Is considered the "Father of Humanism" for his devotion to classical literature and thought
C) Led a military campaign to unify Italy
D) Was the first Protestant reformer

**36.** The Medici family of Florence influenced the Renaissance primarily through:

A) Military conquest of Italy
B) Their patronage of the arts, scholars, and architects
C) Their religious reforms
D) Their exploration of the Americas

**37.** Michelangelo's works, including the Sistine Chapel ceiling and the statue of David, exemplified:

A) Medieval artistic techniques
B) The Renaissance celebration of human form, beauty, and classical themes
C) Islamic artistic traditions
D) Purely abstract art

**38.** The Northern Renaissance differed from the Italian Renaissance in its greater emphasis on:

A) Classical mythology
B) Christian humanism, moral reform, and detailed realism in art
C) Military themes
D) Rejection of all classical influence

**39.** Erasmus of Rotterdam is best known for:

A) Leading the Protestant Reformation
B) His satirical critique of church corruption and his promotion of Christian humanism
C) Discovering the Americas
D) Inventing the printing press

**40.** The Diet of Worms (1521) is significant because:

A) A new pope was elected
B) Martin Luther refused to recant his writings and was declared an outlaw
C) The Thirty Years' War began
D) Germany was unified

**41.** The Anabaptists were a radical Reformation group that believed in:

A) Infant baptism
B) Adult baptism and separation of church and state
C) Papal authority
D) Military crusades

**42.** The Society of Jesus (Jesuits), founded by Ignatius of Loyola, played a key role in:

A) Starting the Protestant Reformation
B) The Counter-Reformation through education, missionary work, and defense of Catholic doctrine
C) The fall of the Byzantine Empire
D) The French Revolution

**43.** The St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre of 1572 involved:

A) A battle between England and Spain
B) The killing of thousands of French Huguenots (Protestants) by Catholics
C) An attack on the Ottoman Empire
D) A peasant revolt in Germany

**44.** The Spanish Armada of 1588 was:

A) A successful invasion of England
B) A large fleet sent by Spain to invade England, which was defeated
C) A trading expedition to the Americas
D) A religious pilgrimage

**45.** The economic system of mercantilism held that:

A) Free trade benefited all nations
B) A nation's wealth depended on accumulating gold and silver and maintaining a favorable balance of trade
C) Government should not interfere in economic matters
D) Agriculture was the only source of wealth

**46.** The transatlantic slave trade was a major element of the:

A) Silk Road trade
B) Triangular trade connecting Europe, Africa, and the Americas
C) Mediterranean spice trade
D) Fur trade in North America

**47.** The Aztec Empire, encountered by Spanish conquistadors, was centered in:

A) South America
B) Central Mexico
C) The Caribbean
D) North America

**48.** The destruction of the Inca Empire was carried out primarily by:

A) British explorers
B) Francisco Pizarro and Spanish conquistadors
C) Portuguese merchants
D) French missionaries

**49.** Francis Bacon's contribution to the Scientific Revolution was:

A) The discovery of gravity
B) The development of the empirical method emphasizing observation and experimentation
C) The heliocentric theory
D) The invention of the telescope

**50.** René Descartes' famous declaration "I think, therefore I am" reflected:

A) A rejection of all knowledge
B) The use of systematic doubt and reason as the foundation of knowledge
C) A religious mystical experience
D) An endorsement of medieval scholasticism

**51.** The English Civil War (1642-1651) was fought between:

A) England and France
B) Royalists (Cavaliers) supporting King Charles I and Parliamentarians (Roundheads)
C) Catholics and Protestants exclusively
D) England and Scotland

**52.** The execution of King Charles I in 1649 was significant because it:

A) Had no lasting impact
B) Demonstrated that a king could be held accountable and executed by his own subjects
C) Led to the immediate restoration of monarchy
D) Ended all conflict in England

**53.** The Glorious Revolution of 1688 in England resulted in:

A) A bloody civil war
B) The peaceful replacement of James II with William and Mary, establishing constitutional monarchy
C) The establishment of a republic
D) French control of England

**54.** The English Bill of Rights (1689) established:

A) Absolute monarchy
B) Parliamentary supremacy and limits on royal power, including free elections and freedom of speech in Parliament
C) The divine right of kings
D) A theocratic government

**55.** Louis XIV of France exemplified absolute monarchy through:

A) Sharing power with the French Parliament
B) Centralizing power in his court at Versailles and famously declaring "L'état, c'est moi"
C) Promoting democracy
D) Allowing religious freedom throughout France

**56.** Peter the Great of Russia sought to:

A) Isolate Russia from Europe
B) Modernize and westernize Russia, building a new capital at St. Petersburg
C) Establish a democratic republic
D) Conquer all of Asia

**57.** The War of the Spanish Succession (1701-1714) was fought primarily over:

A) Religious differences
B) Who would inherit the Spanish throne and the balance of power in Europe
C) Colonial territories in Africa
D) Trade routes to Asia

**58.** The development of the scientific method during the Scientific Revolution emphasized:

A) Authority of ancient texts
B) Systematic observation, hypothesis, experimentation, and conclusion
C) Religious revelation as the source of knowledge
D) Intuition over evidence

**59.** The microscope and telescope, developed in the early 17th century, were significant because they:

A) Were only used for entertainment
B) Extended human observation, enabling discoveries in biology and astronomy
C) Replaced all previous scientific instruments
D) Were invented by a single person

**60.** William Harvey's discovery of the circulation of blood (1628) was important because it:

A) Confirmed ancient Greek medical theories
B) Overturned the prevailing understanding of the body and demonstrated the value of empirical investigation in medicine
C) Was immediately accepted by all physicians
D) Had no impact on medical practice
