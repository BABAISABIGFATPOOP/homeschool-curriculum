# CLEP Western Civilization II (1648 to Present) — Practice Exam 2

**Time Limit:** 90 minutes
**Number of Questions:** 120
**Format:** Multiple choice (A, B, C, D)
**Passing Score:** Typically 50 (on CLEP's 20–80 scale); check with your institution.

## Questions

### Absolutism and Enlightenment (Questions 1–25)

**1.** The divine right of kings asserted that:

A) Kings were elected by the people
B) Monarchs received their authority directly from God and were accountable only to God
C) Parliament had supreme power
D) The Church appointed kings

**2.** Louis XIV's revocation of the Edict of Nantes (1685) resulted in:

A) Religious freedom expanding
B) The persecution and emigration of thousands of Huguenots, weakening the French economy
C) Protestant victory
D) Peace with England

**3.** Jean-Baptiste Colbert, Louis XIV's finance minister, promoted:

A) Free trade
B) Mercantilism — building up French industry, creating state-run manufacturing, and increasing exports
C) Agricultural reform
D) Military reduction

**4.** The War of the Spanish Succession (1701–1714) was fought to prevent:

A) French expansion into Germany
B) The union of the French and Spanish crowns under one Bourbon ruler, which would upset the European balance of power
C) British colonization
D) Ottoman expansion

**5.** The Treaty of Utrecht (1713) established:

A) French dominance of Europe
B) A new balance of power: Britain gained Gibraltar and colonial territories, and the French and Spanish thrones could never be united
C) Spanish dominance
D) German unification

**6.** Frederick William I of Prussia (the "Soldier King") built:

A) A democracy
B) An efficient bureaucracy and one of Europe's best-trained armies, establishing Prussian military tradition
C) Magnificent palaces
D) A colonial empire

**7.** Frederick the Great of Prussia demonstrated "enlightened despotism" by:

A) Establishing democracy
B) Reforming the legal system, promoting religious tolerance, encouraging education, while maintaining absolute power and military expansion
C) Abolishing the army
D) Becoming a constitutional monarch

**8.** The Partitions of Poland (1772, 1793, 1795) by Russia, Prussia, and Austria:

A) Strengthened Poland
B) Gradually eliminated Poland as an independent state, dividing its territory among three empires
C) Were peaceful
D) Were reversed quickly

**9.** The social contract theory held that:

A) Kings ruled by divine right
B) Government is an agreement between the people and their rulers, with the people retaining the right to change unjust government
C) Only the wealthy should govern
D) Government is unnecessary

**10.** Cesare Beccaria's "On Crimes and Punishments" (1764) advocated for:

A) Harsher punishments
B) Proportional punishment, the abolition of torture, and the end of capital punishment
C) More prisons
D) Military courts

**11.** Denis Diderot's Encyclopedia aimed to:

A) Support the Church
B) Compile all human knowledge, promote reason, and challenge superstition and tyranny
C) Replace the Bible
D) Entertain readers

**12.** The physiocrats were Enlightenment thinkers who believed:

A) Manufacturing was the key to wealth
B) Agriculture was the source of all wealth, and government should practice laissez-faire economics
C) Trade should be banned
D) Only gold and silver constituted wealth

**13.** The philosophes believed that society could be improved through:

A) Religious orthodoxy
B) The application of reason, science, and education to human problems
C) Military conquest
D) Return to feudalism

**14.** Enlightenment ideas about religious toleration were partly inspired by:

A) The Crusades
B) The devastating Wars of Religion and thinkers like Voltaire who criticized religious persecution
C) Church policy
D) Military victories

**15.** The concept of natural law in Enlightenment thought held that:

A) Nature is chaotic
B) Universal moral principles discoverable through reason govern human behavior, independent of any government or religion
C) Only religious law is valid
D) There are no universal principles

**16.** Baron d'Holbach represented the radical Enlightenment by advocating:

A) Moderate reform
B) Atheism, materialism, and the idea that religion was the source of humanity's problems
C) Monarchy
D) Religious revival

**17.** Joseph II of Austria was perhaps the most radical enlightened despot because he:

A) Did nothing
B) Abolished serfdom, promoted religious toleration (including for Jews), and reformed education — though many reforms were reversed after his death
C) Strengthened feudalism
D) Declared war on Prussia

**18.** The Scottish Enlightenment produced thinkers like:

A) Only Voltaire
B) David Hume, Adam Smith, and Adam Ferguson, who made major contributions to philosophy, economics, and social science
C) Only military strategists
D) Only poets

**19.** Immanuel Kant's essay "What Is Enlightenment?" (1784) argued that:

A) Enlightenment was impossible
B) Enlightenment means having the courage to use one's own reason — "dare to know" (sapere aude)
C) Only scholars could be enlightened
D) Enlightenment was dangerous

**20.** The coffeehouses and reading clubs of the 18th century were important because they:

A) Only served coffee
B) Created public spaces where people discussed ideas, politics, and culture, fostering the Enlightenment's "public sphere"
C) Were exclusively for the wealthy
D) Were government institutions

**21.** The agricultural revolution of the 18th century included innovations such as:

A) Only better plows
B) Crop rotation, selective breeding, enclosure of common lands, and new farming equipment, increasing food production
C) Returning to medieval methods
D) Banning all farming

**22.** The enclosure movement in England:

A) Benefited all farmers equally
B) Consolidated small farms into larger, more efficient plots but displaced many peasants, pushing them toward cities and factory work

D) Had no effect on urbanization

**24.** The development of the scientific method during this period emphasized:

A) Reliance on ancient authorities
B) Systematic observation, hypothesis, experimentation, and evidence-based conclusions

**25.** Thomas Hobbes's "Leviathan" argued for:
