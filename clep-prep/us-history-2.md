# US History II CLEP Study Guide
## 1865 to Present

## Exam Overview

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Format** | Multiple choice |
| **Number of Questions** | 120 |
| **Time Limit** | 90 minutes |
| **Passing Score** | ~50 out of 80 (scaled); approximately 50% correct |
| **College Credit** | Typically 3 semester hours |
| **Note** | Covers from Reconstruction through the early 21st century |

This exam tests knowledge of American history from the end of the Civil War to modern times. It covers political, social, economic, cultural, and diplomatic developments.

---

## Topics Covered

### 1. Reconstruction (5%)

- **Presidential Reconstruction** (Lincoln/Johnson): lenient toward South
- **Radical Reconstruction** (Congress): military districts, Black male suffrage
- **13th Amendment** (1865): abolished slavery
- **14th Amendment** (1868): citizenship, equal protection, due process
- **15th Amendment** (1870): right to vote regardless of race
- **Freedmen's Bureau**: education, aid for formerly enslaved people
- **Black Codes and Jim Crow**: restrictions on African American rights
- **Compromise of 1877**: ended Reconstruction, removed federal troops from the South
- **Sharecropping and tenant farming**: economic dependence replacing slavery

### 2. Gilded Age (1870s-1900) (13%)

#### Industrialization
- **Second Industrial Revolution**: steel (Bessemer process), oil (Rockefeller/Standard Oil), railroads (Vanderbilt), finance (Carnegie, Morgan)
- **Transcontinental Railroad** (1869): linked East and West
- **Robber barons vs. captains of industry**: debate over industrialists' legacy
- **Vertical and horizontal integration**: controlling supply chain vs. buying out competitors
- **Trusts and monopolies**: concentrated economic power
- **Laissez-faire economics**: minimal government regulation
- **Social Darwinism**: "survival of the fittest" applied to economics (Herbert Spencer)
- **Gospel of Wealth** (Carnegie): rich have an obligation to use wealth for public good

#### Labor
- **Working conditions**: long hours, low pay, child labor, unsafe factories
- **Labor unions**: Knights of Labor, American Federation of Labor (Samuel Gompers)
- **Strikes**: Great Railroad Strike (1877), Haymarket Affair (1886), Homestead Strike (1892), Pullman Strike (1894)

#### Immigration and Urbanization
- **New immigrants** (1880s-1920s): Southern and Eastern Europeans, Chinese
- **Ellis Island** (East Coast) and **Angel Island** (West Coast)
- **Nativism**: anti-immigrant sentiment; Chinese Exclusion Act (1882)
- **Urbanization**: growth of cities, tenements, political machines (Tammany Hall, Boss Tweed)
- **Settlement houses**: Hull House (Jane Addams) — social services for immigrants

#### The West
- **Homestead Act** (1862): free land for settlers
- **Indian Wars**: Battle of Little Bighorn (1876), Wounded Knee Massacre (1890)
- **Dawes Act** (1887): broke up tribal lands, forced assimilation
- **Closing of the frontier** (1890): Frederick Jackson Turner's "Frontier Thesis"

### 3. Progressive Era (1900-1920) (10%)

- **Muckrakers**: journalists exposing corruption — Upton Sinclair (The Jungle), Ida Tarbell (Standard Oil), Jacob Riis (How the Other Half Lives)
- **Theodore Roosevelt**: trust-busting, Square Deal, conservation, Pure Food and Drug Act, Meat Inspection Act
- **William Howard Taft**: continued some progressive policies, but split with TR
- **Woodrow Wilson**: Federal Reserve Act (1913), Clayton Antitrust Act, Federal Trade Commission
- **Constitutional amendments**:
  - 16th (1913): income tax
  - 17th (1913): direct election of senators
  - 18th (1919): Prohibition
  - 19th (1920): women's suffrage
- **Women's suffrage movement**: Susan B. Anthony, Elizabeth Cady Stanton, Alice Paul, Carrie Chapman Catt
- **NAACP** (1909): W.E.B. Du Bois; fought for racial equality
- **Booker T. Washington vs. W.E.B. Du Bois**: accommodation/vocational training vs. full civil rights/higher education

### 4. World War I (5%)

- **Causes of U.S. entry**: unrestricted submarine warfare (Lusitania, 1915), Zimmermann Telegram (1917)
- **U.S. declared war**: April 1917
- **Home front**: Selective Service Act (draft), war bonds, propaganda, Espionage and Sedition Acts
- **Great Migration**: African Americans moved from rural South to Northern cities for industrial jobs
- **Wilson's Fourteen Points**: self-determination, League of Nations
- **Treaty of Versailles** (1919): harsh terms on Germany; U.S. Senate rejected it (isolationism)
- **Red Scare** (1919-1920): fear of communism, Palmer Raids

### 5. Roaring Twenties (1920-1929) (8%)

- **Economic boom**: mass production, consumer goods, automobiles (Henry Ford, assembly line), advertising
- **Cultural changes**: jazz music (Harlem Renaissance), flappers, radio, movies
- **Harlem Renaissance**: African American cultural flowering — Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington
- **Prohibition** (18th Amendment): speakeasies, bootlegging, organized crime
- **Scopes Trial** (1925): evolution vs. creationism in schools
- **Nativism**: Immigration Acts of 1921 and 1924 (quotas), resurgence of KKK
- **Return to normalcy**: Harding, Coolidge, Hoover; pro-business, limited government
- **Speculation**: buying stocks on margin; contributed to the crash

### 6. Great Depression and New Deal (1929-1941) (10%)

#### The Depression
- **Stock market crash**: Black Tuesday (October 29, 1929)
- **Causes**: overproduction, underconsumption, speculation, bank failures, Smoot-Hawley Tariff
- **Effects**: ~25% unemployment, bank failures, Hoovervilles, Dust Bowl (Great Plains ecological disaster)
- **Hoover's response**: initially hands-off; later Reconstruction Finance Corporation, too little too late

#### FDR and the New Deal
- **Election of 1932**: FDR promises a "New Deal"
- **First 100 Days**: flurry of legislation
- **Relief**: CCC (Civilian Conservation Corps), FERA, WPA (jobs programs)
- **Recovery**: AAA (Agricultural Adjustment Act), NRA (National Recovery Administration)
- **Reform**: FDIC (bank insurance), SEC (stock market regulation), Social Security Act (1935), Wagner Act (labor rights)
- **Court-packing scheme**: FDR's failed attempt to add justices to the Supreme Court
- **Critics**: Huey Long ("Share Our Wealth"), Father Coughlin, Supreme Court struck down some programs
- **Legacy**: expanded role of federal government, safety net programs, coalition politics

### 7. World War II (10%)

#### Road to War
- **Isolationism**: Neutrality Acts (1935-1937)
- **Lend-Lease Act** (1941): supplied Allies before U.S. entered
- **Pearl Harbor** (December 7, 1941): Japan attacked; U.S. entered the war

#### The War
- **European Theater**: D-Day (June 6, 1944, Normandy invasion), Battle of the Bulge, V-E Day (May 8, 1945)
- **Pacific Theater**: island hopping, Midway (1942, turning point), Iwo Jima, Okinawa
- **Atomic bombs**: Hiroshima (Aug 6, 1945), Nagasaki (Aug 9, 1945); Japan surrendered (V-J Day)
- **Key leaders**: FDR/Truman, Churchill, Stalin, Eisenhower, MacArthur

#### Home Front
- **War production**: "Arsenal of Democracy," Rosie the Riveter (women in workforce)
- **Japanese American internment**: Executive Order 9066; ~120,000 relocated to camps
- **Double V campaign**: African Americans fought for victory abroad and against racism at home
- **GI Bill** (1944): education and housing benefits for returning veterans
- **Holocaust**: Nazi genocide of 6 million Jews and millions of others

### 8. Cold War (1945-1991) (15%)

#### Origins
- **Iron Curtain**: division of Europe into Western (democratic) and Eastern (communist) blocs
- **Containment**: Truman Doctrine (1947), support nations resisting communism
- **Marshall Plan** (1948): economic aid to rebuild Western Europe
- **NATO** (1949) vs. **Warsaw Pact** (1955): military alliances
- **Berlin Blockade/Airlift** (1948-1949)

#### Key Events
- **Korean War** (1950-1953): containment in Asia; ended in armistice at 38th parallel
- **McCarthyism** (1950s): Red Scare, Senate hearings, blacklists; Senator Joseph McCarthy
- **Arms race and space race**: Sputnik (1957), NASA, moon landing (1969)
- **Cuban Missile Crisis** (1962): closest the world came to nuclear war; Kennedy and Khrushchev
- **Vietnam War** (see next section)
- **Detente** (1970s): relaxation of tensions; Nixon visits China (1972), SALT treaties
- **Reagan and the Cold War**: military buildup, "Evil Empire," SDI ("Star Wars")
- **Fall of the Berlin Wall** (1989) and **collapse of the Soviet Union** (1991)

#### Domestic Cold War Impact
- House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC)
- Federal loyalty oaths
- Nuclear fallout shelters, duck-and-cover drills
- Military-industrial complex (Eisenhower's farewell warning)

### 9. Civil Rights Movement (10%)

- **Brown v. Board of Education** (1954): Supreme Court declared school segregation unconstitutional
- **Montgomery Bus Boycott** (1955-1956): Rosa Parks, MLK Jr. emerged as leader
- **Little Rock Nine** (1957): integration of Central High School; Eisenhower sent troops
- **Sit-ins** (1960): Greensboro, NC; student-led nonviolent protest
- **Freedom Rides** (1961): testing desegregation on interstate buses
- **March on Washington** (1963): "I Have a Dream" speech (MLK Jr.)
- **Civil Rights Act** (1964): banned discrimination in public places and employment
- **Voting Rights Act** (1965): eliminated literacy tests and other barriers to voting
- **Malcolm X**: Black nationalism, Nation of Islam, later moderated views
- **Black Power movement**: Stokely Carmichael, Black Panthers
- **MLK Jr. assassinated** (April 4, 1968)
- **Other movements**: Chicano movement (Cesar Chavez, UFW), American Indian Movement (AIM), women's liberation (Betty Friedan, NOW, Title IX)

### 10. Vietnam War (5%)

- **Background**: French Indochina, Geneva Accords (1954), division at 17th parallel
- **Gulf of Tonkin Resolution** (1964): gave LBJ broad war powers
- **Escalation under LBJ**: troop buildup, draft, bombing campaigns
- **Tet Offensive** (1968): turning point in public opinion; showed the war was not nearly won
- **Anti-war movement**: college protests, Kent State (1970, 4 students killed by National Guard)
- **Nixon**: Vietnamization (gradual withdrawal), expanded bombing, secret bombing of Cambodia
- **Pentagon Papers** (1971): revealed government deception about the war
- **Paris Peace Accords** (1973): U.S. withdrawal
- **Fall of Saigon** (1975): South Vietnam fell to communism
- **Legacy**: 58,000+ American deaths, War Powers Act (1973), lasting distrust of government

### 11. Modern Era (1970s-Present) (9%)

#### 1970s
- **Nixon**: detente, EPA established, Watergate scandal (1972-1974), resignation (Aug 1974)
- **Ford**: pardoned Nixon, economic stagflation
- **Carter**: Camp David Accords (1978, Israel-Egypt peace), Iran Hostage Crisis (1979-1981), energy crisis

#### 1980s
- **Reagan**: Reaganomics (tax cuts, deregulation, supply-side economics), Cold War hawk, Iran-Contra scandal
- **Fall of communism in Eastern Europe** (1989)

#### 1990s
- **George H.W. Bush**: Gulf War (1991), end of Cold War
- **Clinton**: economic prosperity, NAFTA, welfare reform, impeachment (acquitted)
- **Technology boom**: internet, personal computers

#### 2000s and Beyond
- **George W. Bush**: September 11 attacks (2001), War on Terror, Afghanistan, Iraq War (2003), Patriot Act, Hurricane Katrina (2005), Great Recession (2008)
- **Obama**: first African American president, Affordable Care Act, economic recovery

---

## Study Tips

1. Focus heavily on the Cold War (15%) and the Gilded Age (13%) — they are the largest sections.
2. Know the New Deal programs and their categories (Relief, Recovery, Reform).
3. Understand cause-and-effect chains (e.g., Depression → New Deal → expanded government).
4. Be familiar with key Supreme Court cases (Brown v. Board, Plessy v. Ferguson).
5. Connect domestic events to foreign policy (e.g., Cold War fears → McCarthyism).
6. Know the constitutional amendments and their dates, especially 13th-19th.

## Practice Questions

**1.** The Gilded Age term, coined by Mark Twain, refers to a period characterized by:

A) Great social equality
B) Surface prosperity hiding underlying problems like corruption and inequality
C) Territorial expansion in the Pacific
D) The rise of the civil rights movement

**2.** Which New Deal program provided insurance for bank deposits?

A) SEC
B) CCC
C) FDIC
D) WPA

**3.** The Truman Doctrine (1947) was primarily a policy of:

A) Isolationism
B) Containment of communism
C) Economic aid to rebuild Europe
D) Nuclear disarmament

**4.** The Supreme Court case Brown v. Board of Education (1954) overturned which earlier decision?

A) Marbury v. Madison
B) Dred Scott v. Sandford
C) Plessy v. Ferguson
D) Roe v. Wade

**5.** What event directly led to the United States entering World War II?

A) The sinking of the Lusitania
B) The invasion of Poland
C) The attack on Pearl Harbor
D) The Zimmermann Telegram

**6.** The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964) gave the president authority to:

A) Use military force in Vietnam without a formal declaration of war
B) Send economic aid to South Korea
C) Establish the Peace Corps
D) Negotiate with the Soviet Union

**7.** Which event caused President Nixon to resign?

A) The Iran-Contra affair
B) The Watergate scandal
C) The Pentagon Papers
D) The Bay of Pigs invasion

**8.** The 19th Amendment (1920) granted:

A) Freedom of speech
B) The right to vote regardless of race
C) Women's suffrage
D) Prohibition of alcohol

**9.** Japanese American internment during World War II was authorized by:

A) The Espionage Act
B) Executive Order 9066
C) The Neutrality Acts
D) The Lend-Lease Act

**10.** The Tet Offensive (1968) was significant because it:

A) Was a decisive military victory for the United States
B) Turned American public opinion against the Vietnam War
C) Led to the immediate withdrawal of U.S. troops
D) Resulted in a formal declaration of war by Congress

---

## Answer Key

1. **B) Surface prosperity hiding underlying problems** — The Gilded Age was marked by rapid industrial growth on the surface, but underneath were corruption, inequality, poor working conditions, and political machines.
2. **C) FDIC** — The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation insured bank deposits up to a certain amount, restoring public confidence in banks.
3. **B) Containment of communism** — Truman pledged U.S. support for nations resisting communist expansion, starting with aid to Greece and Turkey.
4. **C) Plessy v. Ferguson** — Brown overturned the "separate but equal" doctrine established by Plessy v. Ferguson (1896).
5. **C) The attack on Pearl Harbor** — Japan's surprise attack on December 7, 1941, led Congress to declare war the next day.
6. **A) Use military force in Vietnam without a formal declaration of war** — The resolution gave LBJ virtually unlimited authority to escalate U.S. involvement.
7. **B) The Watergate scandal** — Nixon resigned in August 1974 facing certain impeachment for the cover-up of the Watergate break-in.
8. **C) Women's suffrage** — The 19th Amendment prohibited denying the right to vote based on sex.
9. **B) Executive Order 9066** — Signed by FDR in February 1942, it authorized the forced relocation of approximately 120,000 Japanese Americans to internment camps.
10. **B) Turned American public opinion against the Vietnam War** — Although a military failure for North Vietnam, the scale of the offensive shocked Americans who had been told the war was nearly won.
