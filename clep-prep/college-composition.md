# College Composition CLEP Study Guide

## Exam Overview

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Format** | Multiple choice + 2 essays |
| **Number of Questions** | ~50 multiple choice + 2 essay prompts |
| **Time Limit** | 120 minutes total (~50 min MC, ~70 min essays) |
| **Passing Score** | ~50 out of 80 (scaled); approximately 50% correct |
| **College Credit** | Typically 6 semester hours |
| **Note** | This is the College Composition exam (not Modular). Essays are scored by human readers. |

The College Composition CLEP tests your ability to write clearly and effectively, revise written work, and understand conventions of standard written English.

---

## Topics Covered

### 1. Essay Writing (34% — 2 Essays)

#### Argumentative Essay
- Take a clear position on a given topic
- Develop a thesis statement that is specific and debatable
- Support your argument with evidence, examples, and reasoning
- Address counterarguments
- Organize with introduction, body paragraphs (each with a topic sentence), and conclusion
- Use transitions between paragraphs and ideas

#### Expository Essay
- Explain, analyze, or inform about a topic
- Use a clear organizational pattern (chronological, cause/effect, compare/contrast, etc.)
- Provide specific details and examples
- Maintain an objective, academic tone
- Demonstrate understanding of audience and purpose

**Key Concepts:**
- A strong thesis is specific, arguable, and addresses the prompt directly
- Each body paragraph should have ONE main idea that supports the thesis
- Avoid logical fallacies: ad hominem, straw man, false dichotomy, hasty generalization
- Conclusions should synthesize (not just summarize) and leave the reader with a takeaway
- Budget your time: ~5 min planning, ~25 min writing, ~5 min revising per essay

### 2. Revision Skills (40% of MC)

- **Sentence-level revision**: improving clarity, conciseness, and effectiveness
- **Paragraph-level revision**: improving organization, coherence, and development
- **Eliminating wordiness and redundancy**
- **Improving transitions**: between sentences and paragraphs
- **Strengthening topic sentences**
- **Maintaining consistent tone and point of view**
- **Recognizing and fixing faulty logic**
- **Reorganizing sentences within a paragraph for better flow**

**Key Concepts:**
- Active voice is generally preferred over passive voice (e.g., "The dog bit the man" not "The man was bitten by the dog")
- Parallel structure: items in a series should use the same grammatical form
- Avoid unnecessary shifts in tense, person, or number
- Effective transitions: however, moreover, consequently, in contrast, similarly, therefore
- Conciseness: eliminate "there is/are" constructions, reduce prepositional phrases

### 3. Grammar and Mechanics (25% of MC)

#### Subject-Verb Agreement
- Compound subjects joined by "and" take plural verbs
- Subjects joined by "or/nor" — verb agrees with the nearer subject
- Collective nouns can be singular or plural depending on meaning
- Indefinite pronouns: "everyone, each, nobody" are singular; "few, many, several" are plural
- Ignore intervening phrases between subject and verb

#### Pronoun Usage
- Pronoun-antecedent agreement in number and gender
- Clear pronoun reference (avoid ambiguous "this," "it," "they")
- Subjective case (I, he, she, they) vs. objective case (me, him, her, them)
- Who (subjective) vs. whom (objective)

#### Comma Rules
- Before coordinating conjunctions joining independent clauses (FANBOYS)
- After introductory elements (phrases, clauses)
- Around non-essential (non-restrictive) clauses and appositives
- In a series (serial/Oxford comma may or may not appear)
- Do NOT use a comma to separate a subject from its verb or between two verbs with the same subject

#### Semicolons and Colons
- Semicolon: joins two related independent clauses without a conjunction
- Semicolon: before conjunctive adverbs (however, therefore, moreover) joining clauses
- Colon: introduces a list, explanation, or elaboration after an independent clause

#### Apostrophes
- Possessives: singular (dog's), plural ending in s (dogs'), irregular plural (children's)
- Contractions: it's = it is; who's = who is
- Its (possessive) vs. it's (contraction)

#### Common Errors
- Comma splices: two independent clauses joined by only a comma
- Run-on sentences: two independent clauses with no punctuation
- Sentence fragments: missing a subject, verb, or complete thought
- Dangling and misplaced modifiers
- Double negatives
- Affect (verb) vs. effect (noun, usually)

### 4. Rhetorical Analysis (20% of MC)

- **Purpose**: persuade, inform, entertain, explain
- **Audience awareness**: adjusting tone, diction, and detail for the reader
- **Tone and style**: formal vs. informal, objective vs. subjective
- **Rhetorical strategies**: ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion), logos (logic)
- **Organizational patterns**: cause/effect, compare/contrast, problem/solution, chronological, spatial
- **Identifying thesis and main idea in a passage**
- **Evaluating the effectiveness of evidence and examples**
- **Recognizing bias and assumptions**

**Key Concepts:**
- Ethos = trust/credibility (expert testimony, credentials)
- Pathos = emotional appeal (anecdotes, vivid language)
- Logos = logical appeal (statistics, facts, reasoning)
- An effective argument uses all three but relies primarily on logos

### 5. Research and Citation (10% of MC)

- **Evaluating sources**: credibility, relevance, currency, bias
- **Primary vs. secondary sources**
- **Paraphrasing vs. summarizing vs. quoting**
- **Avoiding plagiarism**: when and how to cite
- **In-text citation basics** (MLA and APA awareness)
- **Integrating quotes smoothly into sentences**
- **Works cited / references page basics**

**Key Concepts:**
- A primary source is original material (diary, speech, experiment data)
- A secondary source analyzes or discusses primary sources (textbook, review article)
- Paraphrase = restate in your own words (still needs citation)
- Direct quotes should be used sparingly and only when exact wording matters
- Evaluate sources with the CRAAP test: Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose

### 6. Sentence Structure (5% of MC)

- **Simple, compound, complex, and compound-complex sentences**
- **Coordination and subordination**
- **Sentence variety for effective writing**
- **Correct use of conjunctions**: coordinating (FANBOYS), subordinating (because, although, when), correlative (both...and, either...or)

**Key Concepts:**
- Simple sentence: one independent clause
- Compound: two independent clauses joined by a conjunction or semicolon
- Complex: one independent + one or more dependent clauses
- Subordination shows the relationship between a main idea and a less important one

---

## Study Tips

1. Read the essay prompts carefully and plan before writing.
2. For the multiple choice, read the entire passage before answering revision questions.
3. Grammar questions often test subject-verb agreement and comma rules — drill these.
4. Practice identifying the BEST answer, not just a correct one (some choices may be partially right).
5. For rhetorical analysis, always ask: What is the author trying to do, and how?
6. Write at least 3-4 practice essays under timed conditions before the exam.

## Practice Questions

**1.** Which sentence contains a comma splice?

A) The storm was fierce, and many trees fell.
B) The storm was fierce, many trees fell.
C) Because the storm was fierce, many trees fell.
D) The storm was fierce; many trees fell.

**2.** Choose the most concise revision: "Due to the fact that it was raining, we decided to stay inside."

A) It was raining outside so that is why we stayed inside the house.
B) Because of the rain that was falling, we made the decision to stay inside.
C) Because it was raining, we stayed inside.
D) We decided to stay inside due to the fact that rain was coming down.

**3.** Which sentence has a dangling modifier?

A) Walking to the store, I saw a rainbow.
B) The dog ran quickly across the yard.
C) After finishing the exam, the classroom emptied.
D) Exhausted from the hike, my bed looked inviting.

**4.** What rhetorical appeal does the following primarily use? "According to Dr. Smith, a leading researcher at Harvard Medical School, this treatment is highly effective."

A) Pathos
B) Logos
C) Ethos
D) Kairos

**5.** Which of the following is a sentence fragment?

A) She ran quickly.
B) Although he wanted to leave early.
C) Stop.
D) The cat sat on the mat.

**6.** Identify the correct sentence:

A) Each of the students need to bring their textbook.
B) Each of the students needs to bring his or her textbook.
C) Each of the students need to bring his or her textbook.
D) Each of the student needs to bring their textbook.

**7.** Which transition best fills the blank? "The experiment yielded positive results. _______, further testing is needed before any conclusions can be drawn."

A) Moreover
B) Nevertheless
C) Similarly
D) For example

**8.** Which is the correct use of a semicolon?

A) The recipe calls for; flour, sugar, and eggs.
B) I love hiking; it is my favorite outdoor activity.
C) She went to the store; and bought milk.
D) He is tall; dark and handsome.

**9.** A student writes: "Texting while driving is dangerous. You could get in an accident. Also people die from it." Which revision skill is most needed?

A) Correcting grammar errors
B) Developing ideas with more specific evidence
C) Fixing punctuation
D) Changing the point of view

**10.** Which source would be considered a primary source for a paper on the Civil War?

A) A history textbook chapter on the Civil War
B) A documentary film about Civil War battles
C) A letter written by a Civil War soldier in 1863
D) A Wikipedia article about the Civil War

---

## Answer Key

1. **B) The storm was fierce, many trees fell.** — Two independent clauses joined by only a comma is a comma splice.
2. **C) Because it was raining, we stayed inside.** — Most concise; eliminates wordiness while preserving meaning.
3. **D) Exhausted from the hike, my bed looked inviting.** — The modifier "exhausted from the hike" illogically modifies "my bed."
4. **C) Ethos** — Citing an expert's credentials (Dr. Smith, Harvard) establishes credibility.
5. **B) Although he wanted to leave early.** — This is a dependent clause with no independent clause to complete it.
6. **B) Each of the students needs to bring his or her textbook.** — "Each" is singular, requiring "needs" and "his or her."
7. **B) Nevertheless** — The second sentence contrasts with the first (positive results, but more testing needed).
8. **B) I love hiking; it is my favorite outdoor activity.** — Correctly joins two related independent clauses.
9. **B) Developing ideas with more specific evidence** — The ideas are vague and underdeveloped; they need statistics, examples, or details.
10. **C) A letter written by a Civil War soldier in 1863** — A primary source is an original document from the period being studied.
