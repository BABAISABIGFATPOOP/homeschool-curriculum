# Western Civilization I CLEP Study Guide
## Ancient Near East to 1648

## Exam Overview

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Format** | Multiple choice |
| **Number of Questions** | 120 |
| **Time Limit** | 90 minutes |
| **Passing Score** | ~50 out of 80 (scaled); approximately 50% correct |
| **College Credit** | Typically 3 semester hours |
| **Note** | Covers from the earliest civilizations through the end of the Thirty Years' War |

This exam covers the development of Western civilization from ancient times through the mid-17th century, including political, economic, social, cultural, and intellectual history.

---

## Topics Covered

### 1. Ancient Near East (8%)

#### Mesopotamia
- **Sumer**: first civilization, cuneiform writing, city-states (Ur, Uruk), ziggurats
- **Akkadian Empire** (Sargon): first empire in history
- **Babylonian Empire**: Hammurabi's Code — one of the earliest written legal codes ("eye for an eye"); codified social hierarchy
- **Assyrian Empire**: military power, brutal conquests, iron weapons
- **Neo-Babylonian Empire**: Nebuchadnezzar II, Hanging Gardens, conquered Judah

#### Egypt
- **Nile River**: annual flooding, fertile soil, "gift of the Nile"
- **Pharaoh**: god-king, absolute authority
- **Old Kingdom**: Great Pyramids at Giza, centralized power
- **Middle Kingdom**: expansion, trade
- **New Kingdom**: height of power (Hatshepsut, Akhenaten, Tutankhamun, Ramses II)
- **Religion**: polytheistic, afterlife beliefs, mummification, Book of the Dead
- **Hieroglyphics**: writing system; Rosetta Stone key to decipherment

#### Other Civilizations
- **Phoenicians**: alphabet (basis for Greek/Latin alphabets), maritime trade, purple dye
- **Hebrews/Israelites**: monotheism (Judaism), Torah, covenant with God, Moses, Ten Commandments, Babylonian Captivity
- **Persians**: Cyrus the Great (tolerant rule), Darius I (organized empire into satrapies), Royal Road, Zoroastrianism

**Key Concepts:**
- River valley civilizations developed writing, law, organized religion, and social hierarchy
- Code of Hammurabi established the principle that laws should be publicly known
- Hebrew monotheism profoundly influenced Western religious thought

### 2. Ancient Greece (15%)

#### Minoan and Mycenaean Civilizations
- **Minoans** (Crete): palace at Knossos, maritime trade, fell ~1450 BC
- **Mycenaeans** (mainland Greece): warrior culture, influenced by Minoans, Trojan War era
- **Greek Dark Ages** (~1100-800 BC): loss of writing, decline of trade

#### Archaic and Classical Greece
- **Polis (city-state)**: fundamental Greek political unit
- **Athens**: direct democracy (for male citizens), Solon's reforms, Cleisthenes' democratic reforms, Assembly, jury system
- **Sparta**: military oligarchy, dual kings, helots (enslaved people), rigid social structure, emphasis on military training
- **Persian Wars** (490-479 BC): Marathon (490), Thermopylae (480, Spartans), Salamis (480), Plataea (479); Greeks defeated the Persian Empire
- **Delian League**: Athenian-led alliance, became Athenian Empire
- **Golden Age of Athens** (Pericles): democracy, Parthenon, art, drama, philosophy
- **Peloponnesian War** (431-404 BC): Athens vs. Sparta; Sparta won; weakened all Greek city-states

#### Greek Culture and Philosophy
- **Philosophy**: Socrates (questioning, Socratic method), Plato (The Republic, Theory of Forms, ideal state), Aristotle (logic, empiricism, politics, ethics)
- **Drama**: tragedy (Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides) and comedy (Aristophanes)
- **History**: Herodotus ("Father of History"), Thucydides (scientific history, Peloponnesian War)
- **Science/Math**: Pythagoras, Hippocrates (medicine), Archimedes
- **Art/Architecture**: idealized human form, columns (Doric, Ionic, Corinthian), Parthenon
- **Religion**: polytheistic, Olympian gods, oracles, festivals (Olympics)

#### Hellenistic Period (323-31 BC)
- **Alexander the Great**: conquered Persian Empire, spread Greek culture from Egypt to India
- **Hellenistic kingdoms**: Ptolemaic Egypt, Seleucid Empire, Antigonid Macedonia
- **Cultural blending**: Greek culture mixed with Eastern traditions
- **Philosophy**: Stoicism (virtue, duty, reason), Epicureanism (pleasure = absence of pain), Cynicism
- **Science**: Euclid (geometry), Archimedes, Eratosthenes (circumference of Earth)
- **Library of Alexandria**: center of learning

### 3. Ancient Rome (15%)

#### Roman Republic (509-27 BC)
- **Government**: Senate, consuls (2), tribunes (represented plebeians), Twelve Tables (written law)
- **Patricians vs. Plebeians**: class struggle; Conflict of the Orders led to more plebeian rights
- **Punic Wars** (264-146 BC): Rome vs. Carthage; Hannibal crossed the Alps; Rome destroyed Carthage
- **Expansion**: conquered Mediterranean, Greek culture absorbed
- **Crisis of the Republic**: land reform (Gracchi brothers), military reforms (Marius), civil wars (Sulla, Pompey, Caesar)
- **Julius Caesar**: crossed the Rubicon (49 BC), dictator, assassinated (44 BC, Ides of March)
- **Fall of the Republic**: power struggles, Second Triumvirate (Octavian, Antony, Lepidus)

#### Roman Empire (27 BC - 476 AD)
- **Augustus (Octavian)**: first emperor, Pax Romana begins (27 BC - 180 AD), reformed government, patronized arts
- **Pax Romana**: ~200 years of relative peace and prosperity; roads, trade, law
- **Roman law**: innocent until proven guilty, right to a defense; foundation of Western legal systems
- **Engineering**: aqueducts, roads, Colosseum, concrete, arches
- **Literature**: Virgil (Aeneid), Ovid, Horace, Cicero
- **Decline**: overexpansion, military spending, political instability, barbarian invasions, economic troubles, division into East and West (285 AD, Diocletian)
- **Constantine**: Edict of Milan (313, legalized Christianity), moved capital to Constantinople
- **Theodosius**: made Christianity the official religion (380)
- **Fall of Western Rome** (476 AD): last emperor deposed by Germanic chief Odoacer

### 4. Early Christianity (5%)

- **Jesus of Nazareth**: teachings of love, forgiveness, salvation; crucified under Pontius Pilate (~30 AD)
- **Paul of Tarsus**: spread Christianity to Gentiles; missionary journeys; epistles
- **Persecution**: intermittent Roman persecution; Christians as scapegoats (Nero)
- **Constantine and the Edict of Milan** (313): toleration of Christianity
- **Council of Nicaea** (325): established core Christian doctrine (Nicene Creed)
- **Early Church Fathers**: Augustine (City of God, original sin), Jerome (Vulgate Bible)
- **Monasticism**: Benedict of Nursia — Benedictine Rule (ora et labora — pray and work)
- **Pope**: Bishop of Rome; growing authority in the West

### 5. Byzantine Empire (5%)

- **Eastern Roman Empire**: survived the fall of the West by nearly 1,000 years
- **Constantinople**: capital, strategic location, wealthy trade center
- **Justinian** (527-565): Justinian's Code (compiled Roman law), reconquered parts of the West, Hagia Sophia
- **Greek culture and Orthodox Christianity**: distinct from Latin/Catholic West
- **Iconoclasm controversy**: debate over religious images
- **Great Schism** (1054): split between Roman Catholic (West) and Eastern Orthodox (East) churches
- **Fall of Constantinople** (1453): conquered by Ottoman Turks; end of the Byzantine Empire

### 6. Medieval Europe (15%)

#### Early Middle Ages (500-1000)
- **Germanic kingdoms**: Franks (most successful), Visigoths, Ostrogoths, Anglo-Saxons
- **Clovis**: Frankish king, converted to Christianity (496)
- **Charlemagne** (768-814): united much of Western Europe, crowned Holy Roman Emperor by the Pope (800), Carolingian Renaissance
- **Viking, Magyar, and Muslim invasions**: destabilized Europe in the 9th-10th centuries
- **Feudalism**: political/military system — king → lords → vassals → serfs; based on land grants (fiefs) in exchange for loyalty and military service
- **Manorialism**: economic system; self-sufficient estates; serfs worked the land

#### High Middle Ages (1000-1300)
- **Catholic Church**: dominant institution; Pope vs. kings; excommunication, interdict
- **Investiture Controversy** (1075-1122): Pope Gregory VII vs. Emperor Henry IV over appointment of church officials; Concordat of Worms
- **Crusades** (1095-1291): holy wars to recapture the Holy Land from Muslims
  - First Crusade (1096-1099): captured Jerusalem
  - Effects: increased trade, cultural exchange, weakened feudalism, strengthened monarchies
- **Growth of towns and trade**: merchant class (bourgeoisie), guilds, banking, Hanseatic League
- **Universities**: Bologna, Paris, Oxford; scholasticism (Thomas Aquinas — faith and reason compatible)
- **Gothic architecture**: cathedrals (Notre-Dame, Chartres), pointed arches, flying buttresses, stained glass
- **Magna Carta** (1215): King John forced to sign; limited royal power, due process, foundation for constitutionalism

#### Late Middle Ages (1300-1500)
- **Black Death** (1347-1351): killed ~1/3 of Europe's population; labor shortages, social upheaval, questioning of the Church
- **Hundred Years' War** (1337-1453): England vs. France; Joan of Arc; French victory; national identity
- **Great Schism** (1378-1417): multiple competing popes; weakened Church authority
- **Decline of feudalism**: due to plague, war, rise of centralized monarchies

### 7. Islam (5%)

- **Muhammad**: prophet (570-632), Quran (holy book), Five Pillars of Islam
- **Five Pillars**: shahada (faith), salat (prayer), zakat (charity), sawm (fasting during Ramadan), hajj (pilgrimage to Mecca)
- **Rapid expansion**: Arabian Peninsula, North Africa, Spain, Persia, parts of India
- **Umayyad and Abbasid Caliphates**: political and cultural centers
- **Islamic Golden Age**: preserved and advanced Greek/Roman knowledge; advances in algebra, medicine, astronomy, optics
- **Key figures**: Avicenna (Ibn Sina, medicine), Averroes (Ibn Rushd, philosophy/Aristotle), al-Khwarizmi (algebra)
- **Al-Andalus**: Muslim Spain; coexistence of Muslims, Christians, Jews; Cordoba as cultural center
- **Sunni-Shia split**: dispute over Muhammad's successor

### 8. Renaissance (12%)

- **Italian Renaissance** (14th-16th centuries): rebirth of classical Greek and Roman culture
- **Humanism**: focus on human potential, secular subjects, classical texts; "man is the measure of all things"
- **Key humanists**: Petrarch ("Father of Humanism"), Machiavelli (The Prince — political realism), Erasmus (Christian humanism, In Praise of Folly), Thomas More (Utopia)
- **Italian city-states**: Florence (Medici patronage), Venice, Rome, Milan
- **Art**:
  - Leonardo da Vinci: Mona Lisa, Last Supper; scientist and artist
  - Michelangelo: Sistine Chapel ceiling, David, St. Peter's Basilica
  - Raphael: School of Athens
  - Techniques: perspective, chiaroscuro, anatomical accuracy
- **Printing press** (Gutenberg, ~1440): revolutionized knowledge distribution, enabled Reformation
- **Northern Renaissance**: Erasmus, Thomas More, Shakespeare, Albrecht Durer; more Christian and social focus
- **Scientific developments**: Copernicus (heliocentric theory), Galileo (telescope, supported Copernicism), Vesalius (anatomy)

### 9. Reformation (10%)

#### Protestant Reformation
- **Causes**: corruption in the Catholic Church (simony, nepotism, pluralism), sale of indulgences, Renaissance humanism encouraged critical thinking
- **Martin Luther** (1517): 95 Theses, challenged indulgences; sola fide (faith alone), sola scriptura (scripture alone), priesthood of all believers
- **Diet of Worms** (1521): Luther refused to recant; excommunicated
- **Lutheranism**: spread through German states; Peace of Augsburg (1555) — rulers choose religion (cuius regio, eius religio)
- **John Calvin**: predestination, Geneva as "city of God," Calvinist work ethic
- **Henry VIII and the English Reformation**: broke from Rome over annulment; Act of Supremacy (1534); Church of England (Anglican)
- **Anabaptists**: adult baptism, separation of church and state
- **Radical Reformation**: various sects seeking deeper reform

#### Catholic (Counter) Reformation
- **Council of Trent** (1545-1563): reaffirmed Catholic doctrine, reformed abuses, clarified teachings
- **Jesuits** (Society of Jesus, founded by Ignatius of Loyola): education, missionary work, Counter-Reformation champions
- **Inquisition**: strengthened to combat heresy
- **Baroque art**: emotional, dramatic style promoting Catholic faith

#### Wars of Religion
- **French Wars of Religion** (1562-1598): Catholics vs. Huguenots (French Calvinists); St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (1572); Edict of Nantes (1598, Henry IV granted tolerance)
- **Thirty Years' War** (1618-1648): began as religious conflict in Holy Roman Empire, became a political struggle
  - Phases: Bohemian, Danish, Swedish, French
  - **Peace of Westphalia** (1648): ended the war; established state sovereignty, weakened Holy Roman Emperor, recognized Calvinism

---

## Study Tips

1. Create a timeline from ancient civilizations through 1648 — many questions test chronology.
2. Focus on Greece and Rome (30% combined) — these are the largest sections.
3. Know the major philosophical and cultural contributions of each period.
4. Understand the causes and effects of the Reformation.
5. Be able to compare civilizations (e.g., Athens vs. Sparta, Roman Republic vs. Empire).
6. Pay attention to the Catholic Church's role throughout the medieval period.

## Practice Questions

**1.** Hammurabi's Code is significant in Western history because it:

A) Established monotheism
B) Was one of the earliest written legal codes
C) Created the first democratic government
D) Invented the alphabet

**2.** Which Greek philosopher is known for the Socratic method of questioning?

A) Plato
B) Aristotle
C) Socrates
D) Epicurus

**3.** The Pax Romana refers to a period of:

A) Greek cultural dominance
B) Religious warfare
C) Approximately 200 years of Roman peace and stability
D) The decline of the Roman Empire

**4.** What was the primary cause of the Great Schism of 1054?

A) The Protestant Reformation
B) The Crusades
C) Theological and political differences between the Eastern and Western churches
D) The fall of Constantinople

**5.** The Magna Carta (1215) is important because it:

A) Established the Church of England
B) Limited the power of the English king
C) Ended the Hundred Years' War
D) Started the Crusades

**6.** Which of the following best describes humanism during the Renaissance?

A) Rejection of all religious belief
B) Focus on human potential and classical learning
C) Strict adherence to medieval traditions
D) Emphasis on military conquest

**7.** Martin Luther's 95 Theses (1517) primarily challenged:

A) The authority of the Bible
B) The sale of indulgences by the Catholic Church
C) The existence of God
D) The power of German princes

**8.** The Peace of Westphalia (1648) is significant because it:

A) Ended the Crusades
B) Unified the Holy Roman Empire
C) Established the principle of state sovereignty
D) Created the Protestant Reformation

**9.** Which empire preserved Roman law through Justinian's Code?

A) The Carolingian Empire
B) The Ottoman Empire
C) The Byzantine Empire
D) The Holy Roman Empire

**10.** The Black Death (1347-1351) had which major effect on European society?

A) It strengthened the feudal system
B) It caused severe labor shortages and social upheaval
C) It increased the power of the Catholic Church
D) It ended the Hundred Years' War

---

## Answer Key

1. **B) Was one of the earliest written legal codes** — Hammurabi's Code (c. 1754 BC) established publicly known laws, an important precedent for Western legal tradition.
2. **C) Socrates** — Socrates developed the method of asking probing questions to stimulate critical thinking and expose contradictions.
3. **C) Approximately 200 years of Roman peace and stability** — The Pax Romana (27 BC - 180 AD) was a period of relative peace, prosperity, and expansion across the Roman Empire.
4. **C) Theological and political differences between the Eastern and Western churches** — Disputes over papal authority, the filioque clause, and cultural differences led to the permanent split.
5. **B) Limited the power of the English king** — The Magna Carta established that the king was subject to law, laid groundwork for due process and constitutionalism.
6. **B) Focus on human potential and classical learning** — Humanism emphasized studying classical Greek and Roman texts and celebrated human achievement, while not necessarily rejecting religion.
7. **B) The sale of indulgences by the Catholic Church** — Luther objected to the selling of indulgences (payments to reduce punishment for sins), which he saw as corrupt.
8. **C) Established the principle of state sovereignty** — Westphalia ended the Thirty Years' War and established that each state had sovereignty over its territory and religion.
9. **C) The Byzantine Empire** — Emperor Justinian commissioned the Corpus Juris Civilis, which compiled and organized centuries of Roman law.
10. **B) It caused severe labor shortages and social upheaval** — With roughly a third of Europe's population dead, surviving laborers could demand higher wages, weakening feudalism.
