## Topics Covered

### 1. Molecular and Cellular Biology (33%)

#### Chemistry of Life
- **Water**: polarity, hydrogen bonding, cohesion, adhesion, high specific heat, universal solvent
- **pH scale**: acids (0-6.9), neutral (7), bases (7.1-14); buffers resist pH changes
- **Organic molecules**:
  - Carbohydrates: monosaccharides, disaccharides, polysaccharides (starch, glycogen, cellulose)
  - Lipids: fats (saturated/unsaturated), phospholipids, steroids
  - Proteins: amino acids, peptide bonds, 4 levels of structure (primary through quaternary)
  - Nucleic acids: DNA (deoxyribose, double-stranded) and RNA (ribose, single-stranded)
- **Enzymes**: biological catalysts, lower activation energy, substrate-specific (lock and key / induced fit), affected by temperature, pH, and concentration

#### Cell Structure
- **Prokaryotic vs. eukaryotic**: prokaryotes lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles
- **Plant vs. animal cells**: plants have cell walls, chloroplasts, large central vacuole
- **Organelles and their functions**:
  - Nucleus: contains DNA, site of transcription
  - Ribosome: protein synthesis
  - Rough ER: protein processing (has ribosomes)
  - Smooth ER: lipid synthesis, detoxification
  - Golgi apparatus: modification, packaging, shipping of proteins
  - Mitochondria: cellular respiration (ATP production)
  - Chloroplast: photosynthesis (plants/algae)
  - Lysosome: digestion of waste
  - Cell membrane: phospholipid bilayer, selectively permeable
- **Fluid mosaic model**: membrane structure with embedded proteins

#### Cell Transport
- **Passive transport** (no energy required): diffusion, osmosis, facilitated diffusion
- **Active transport** (requires ATP): sodium-potassium pump, endocytosis, exocytosis
- **Osmosis**: water moves from low solute to high solute concentration
- **Tonicity**: hypertonic, hypotonic, isotonic solutions

#### Cellular Respiration
- **Overall equation**: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP (+ heat)
- **Glycolysis**: cytoplasm, glucose → 2 pyruvate, net 2 ATP, 2 NADH
- **Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle)**: mitochondrial matrix, produces CO₂, NADH, FADH₂, 2 ATP
- **Electron transport chain (ETC)**: inner mitochondrial membrane, produces ~34 ATP via oxidative phosphorylation
- **Total yield**: ~36-38 ATP per glucose
- **Anaerobic respiration**: fermentation (lactic acid in animals, ethanol in yeast)

#### Photosynthesis
- **Overall equation**: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
- **Light reactions**: thylakoid membranes, water split, O₂ released, ATP and NADPH produced
- **Calvin cycle (dark reactions)**: stroma, CO₂ fixed into glucose using ATP and NADPH
- **Photosystems I and II**: contain chlorophyll, capture light energy
- **C3, C4, and CAM plants**: adaptations for different environments

#### Cell Division
- **Cell cycle**: G1, S (DNA replication), G2, M (mitosis)
- **Mitosis**: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase — produces 2 identical diploid cells
- **Meiosis**: two divisions, produces 4 haploid gametes, crossing over and independent assortment create genetic variation
- **Key differences**: mitosis = growth/repair (2n → 2n); meiosis = gamete production (2n → n)

### 2. Genetics and Evolution (20%)

#### Molecular Genetics
- **DNA structure**: double helix, nucleotides (A-T, G-C), antiparallel strands
- **DNA replication**: semiconservative, helicase unwinds, DNA polymerase synthesizes new strand
- **Transcription**: DNA → mRNA in the nucleus (RNA polymerase)
- **Translation**: mRNA → protein at ribosomes; codons read by tRNA with anticodons
- **Genetic code**: 64 codons, 20 amino acids, start codon (AUG), stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA)
- **Mutations**: point mutations (substitution, insertion, deletion), frameshift mutations
- **Gene regulation**: operons (lac operon), promoters, transcription factors

#### Mendelian Genetics
- **Mendel's laws**: segregation (alleles separate in meiosis), independent assortment
- **Dominant vs. recessive alleles**: homozygous, heterozygous
- **Punnett squares**: monohybrid and dihybrid crosses
- **Genotype vs. phenotype**
- **Incomplete dominance**: blended phenotype (red x white = pink)
- **Codominance**: both alleles fully expressed (AB blood type)
- **Sex-linked traits**: carried on X chromosome, more common in males
- **Polygenic inheritance**: multiple genes affect one trait (skin color, height)
- **Pedigree analysis**: identifying inheritance patterns in families

#### Evolution
- **Darwin's theory of natural selection**: variation, overproduction, competition, survival of the fittest, reproduction
- **Evidence for evolution**: fossil record, comparative anatomy (homologous, analogous, vestigial structures), embryology, molecular biology (DNA/protein comparisons)
- **Microevolution**: changes in allele frequency within a population
- **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium**: p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (conditions: large population, no mutation, no migration, random mating, no selection)
- **Mechanisms of evolution**: mutation, gene flow, genetic drift (bottleneck, founder effect), natural selection
- **Speciation**: allopatric (geographic isolation) vs. sympatric
- **Adaptive radiation**: rapid diversification from a common ancestor

### 3. Organisms: Diversity, Structure, and Function (27%)

#### Diversity of Life
- **Classification hierarchy**: Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species
- **Three domains**: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya
- **Six kingdoms**: Archaebacteria, Eubacteria, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia
- **Viruses**: not living, require a host cell, DNA or RNA, lytic and lysogenic cycles

#### Plant Biology
- **Plant tissues**: dermal, vascular (xylem and phloem), ground
- **Xylem**: transports water up (transpiration pull); dead cells
- **Phloem**: transports sugars (translocation); living cells
- **Roots, stems, leaves**: structure and function
- **Reproduction**: alternation of generations (sporophyte and gametophyte)
- **Tropisms**: phototropism, gravitropism, thigmotropism
- **Hormones**: auxins, gibberellins, cytokinins, ethylene, abscisic acid

#### Animal Biology
- **Organ systems overview**:
  - Digestive: mouth → esophagus → stomach → small intestine → large intestine
  - Circulatory: heart, blood vessels, blood (open vs. closed systems)
  - Respiratory: lungs (mammals), gills (fish), tracheal system (insects)
  - Nervous: central (brain, spinal cord) and peripheral; neurons, synapses
  - Endocrine: hormones, glands (pituitary, thyroid, adrenal, pancreas)
  - Immune: innate vs. adaptive, B cells (antibodies) vs. T cells
  - Excretory: kidneys, nephrons, filtration
  - Reproductive: sexual vs. asexual, gametes, fertilization
- **Homeostasis**: maintaining stable internal conditions (temperature, pH, glucose)
- **Feedback loops**: negative feedback (most common, maintains stability) vs. positive feedback (amplifies change)

### 4. Ecology and Population Biology (20%)

#### Ecology Basics
- **Levels of organization**: organism, population, community, ecosystem, biome, biosphere
- **Biomes**: tundra, taiga, temperate forest, tropical rainforest, grassland, desert, aquatic
- **Abiotic vs. biotic factors**

#### Energy Flow
- **Food chains and food webs**
- **Trophic levels**: producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, tertiary consumers, decomposers
- **10% rule**: only about 10% of energy transfers between trophic levels
- **Energy pyramids**: always upright; biomass and numbers pyramids can vary

#### Biogeochemical Cycles
- **Water cycle**: evaporation, condensation, precipitation, transpiration, runoff
- **Carbon cycle**: photosynthesis, respiration, combustion, decomposition
- **Nitrogen cycle**: nitrogen fixation, nitrification, denitrification, assimilation
- **Phosphorus cycle**: no atmospheric component, moves through rocks and water

#### Population Ecology
- **Population growth**: exponential (J-curve) vs. logistic (S-curve)
- **Carrying capacity (K)**: maximum population an environment can sustain
- **Density-dependent factors**: competition, predation, disease
- **Density-independent factors**: natural disasters, climate
- **r-selected vs. K-selected species**: many offspring/low parental care vs. few offspring/high parental care

#### Community Ecology
- **Symbiosis**: mutualism (+/+), commensalism (+/0), parasitism (+/-)
- **Competition**: interspecific and intraspecific
- **Predator-prey relationships**: population oscillations
- **Ecological succession**: primary (bare rock) and secondary (after disturbance)
- **Keystone species**: disproportionate impact on ecosystem relative to their abundance
- **Biodiversity**: species richness and evenness

## Study Tips

1. This is one of the hardest CLEP exams — give yourself plenty of study time.
2. Focus on understanding concepts rather than memorizing isolated facts.
3. Know the processes of photosynthesis and cellular respiration thoroughly (inputs, outputs, locations).
4. Practice Punnett squares and Hardy-Weinberg problems.
5. Be comfortable reading graphs, diagrams, and experimental data.
6. Use flashcards for vocabulary-heavy topics like organelles and organ systems.

## Practice Questions

**1.** Which organelle is the primary site of ATP production in eukaryotic cells?

A) Ribosome
B) Golgi apparatus
C) Mitochondria
D) Chloroplast

**2.** During which phase of meiosis does crossing over occur?

A) Prophase I
B) Metaphase II
C) Anaphase I
D) Telophase II

**3.** What is the correct order of the stages of cellular respiration?

A) Krebs cycle → Glycolysis → Electron transport chain
B) Glycolysis → Electron transport chain → Krebs cycle
C) Glycolysis → Krebs cycle → Electron transport chain
D) Electron transport chain → Glycolysis → Krebs cycle

**4.** In a population at Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, if the frequency of the recessive allele (q) is 0.3, what is the frequency of heterozygous individuals (2pq)?

A) 0.09
B) 0.42
C) 0.49
D) 0.21

**5.** Which of the following is NOT a condition required for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium?

A) Large population size
B) Random mating
C) Natural selection acting on the population
D) No migration

**6.** A cross between a homozygous dominant (AA) and a homozygous recessive (aa) produces offspring that are all:

A) Homozygous dominant
B) Homozygous recessive
C) Heterozygous
D) A mix of dominant and recessive

**7.** Which trophic level has the MOST energy in an ecosystem?

A) Primary consumers
B) Secondary consumers
C) Producers
D) Tertiary consumers

**8.** The relationship between a clownfish and a sea anemone, where both benefit, is an example of:

A) Parasitism
B) Commensalism
C) Mutualism
D) Competition

**9.** What is the role of RNA polymerase in gene expression?

A) It replicates DNA before cell division
B) It translates mRNA into protein
C) It transcribes DNA into mRNA
D) It transports amino acids to the ribosome

**10.** Which type of succession occurs on bare rock where no soil exists?

A) Secondary succession
B) Primary succession
C) Climax succession
D) Regressive succession

---

## Answer Key

1. **C) Mitochondria** — The mitochondria are the "powerhouses of the cell," where oxidative phosphorylation produces most ATP.
2. **A) Prophase I** — Crossing over (exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes) occurs during prophase I of meiosis.
3. **C) Glycolysis → Krebs cycle → Electron transport chain** — Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm, then pyruvate enters the mitochondria for the Krebs cycle and ETC.
4. **B) 0.42** — If q = 0.3, then p = 0.7. 2pq = 2(0.7)(0.3) = 0.42.
5. **C) Natural selection acting on the population** — Hardy-Weinberg requires NO natural selection. The five conditions are: large population, random mating, no mutation, no migration, no selection.
6. **C) Heterozygous** — AA x aa → all Aa offspring (heterozygous), expressing the dominant phenotype.
7. **C) Producers** — Producers (plants/autotrophs) capture energy from the sun. Each subsequent level has only ~10% of the previous level's energy.
8. **C) Mutualism** — Both organisms benefit: the clownfish gets protection, and the anemone gets food scraps and cleaning.
9. **C) It transcribes DNA into mRNA** — RNA polymerase reads the DNA template strand and synthesizes a complementary mRNA strand.
10. **B) Primary succession** — Primary succession begins on surfaces with no prior life or soil, such as bare rock or cooled lava. Pioneer species like lichens colonize first.
