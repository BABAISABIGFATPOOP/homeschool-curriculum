## Topics Covered

### 1. Atomic Theory and Structure (7%)

- **Atomic structure**: protons (positive, in nucleus), neutrons (neutral, in nucleus), electrons (negative, in orbitals)
- **Atomic number**: number of protons (defines the element)
- **Mass number**: protons + neutrons
- **Isotopes**: same element, different number of neutrons
- **Atomic mass**: weighted average of all isotopes
- **Historical models**: Dalton (solid sphere), Thomson (plum pudding), Rutherford (nuclear), Bohr (orbits), quantum mechanical (orbitals/electron cloud)

#### Electron Configuration
- **Aufbau principle**: fill lowest energy orbitals first
- **Pauli exclusion principle**: max 2 electrons per orbital, with opposite spins
- **Hund's rule**: one electron in each orbital of a sublevel before pairing
- **Order of filling**: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p...
- **Orbital types**: s (2e⁻), p (6e⁻), d (10e⁻), f (14e⁻)
- **Valence electrons**: outermost shell electrons; determine chemical behavior
- **Noble gas shorthand**: [Ne] 3s² 3p³ for phosphorus

#### Periodic Trends
- **Atomic radius**: increases down a group, decreases across a period
- **Ionization energy**: energy to remove an electron; increases across a period, decreases down a group
- **Electronegativity**: tendency to attract electrons; increases across a period (F is highest), decreases down a group
- **Electron affinity**: energy change when gaining an electron

### 2. Chemical Bonding (10%)

#### Types of Bonds
- **Ionic bonds**: transfer of electrons (metal + nonmetal), form crystal lattices
- **Covalent bonds**: sharing of electrons (nonmetal + nonmetal)
  - Single, double, triple bonds
  - Polar covalent: unequal sharing (electronegativity difference 0.4-1.7)
  - Nonpolar covalent: equal sharing (electronegativity difference < 0.4)
- **Metallic bonds**: "sea of electrons" shared among metal atoms

#### Lewis Structures
- Count total valence electrons
- Place least electronegative atom in center
- Distribute electrons to satisfy the octet rule (except H: duet)
- Use multiple bonds if needed to complete octets
- **Exceptions to octet**: incomplete octet (BF₃), expanded octet (SF₆, PCl₅)
- **Formal charge**: valence e⁻ - (lone pair e⁻ + 1/2 bonding e⁻)
- **Resonance structures**: multiple valid Lewis structures; actual structure is a hybrid

#### Molecular Geometry (VSEPR Theory)
- 2 electron groups: linear (180°)
- 3 electron groups: trigonal planar (120°), bent (with 1 lone pair)
- 4 electron groups: tetrahedral (109.5°), trigonal pyramidal (1 lone pair), bent (2 lone pairs)
- 5 electron groups: trigonal bipyramidal, seesaw, T-shaped, linear
- 6 electron groups: octahedral, square pyramidal, square planar
- Lone pairs take up more space than bonding pairs

#### Intermolecular Forces (weakest to strongest)
- **London dispersion forces**: present in ALL molecules; stronger with more electrons/larger size
- **Dipole-dipole**: between polar molecules
- **Hydrogen bonding**: special dipole-dipole; H bonded to N, O, or F
- **Ion-dipole**: between ions and polar molecules (in solutions)
- Stronger IMFs = higher boiling point, higher viscosity, higher surface tension

### 3. Stoichiometry (12%)

- **Mole concept**: 1 mol = 6.022 x 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number)
- **Molar mass**: mass of 1 mol of a substance (g/mol), from periodic table
- **Percent composition**: (mass of element / molar mass of compound) x 100
- **Empirical formula**: simplest whole-number ratio of atoms
- **Molecular formula**: actual number of atoms; multiple of empirical formula

#### Balancing Equations and Calculations
- **Law of conservation of mass**: atoms are neither created nor destroyed
- **Mole ratios**: from balanced equation coefficients
- **Mass-to-mass conversions**: grams → moles → mole ratio → moles → grams
- **Limiting reagent**: the reactant that runs out first; determines the maximum product
- **Theoretical yield**: maximum amount of product from the limiting reagent
- **Percent yield**: (actual yield / theoretical yield) x 100

#### Types of Reactions
- **Synthesis (combination)**: A + B → AB
- **Decomposition**: AB → A + B
- **Single replacement**: A + BC → AC + B (use activity series)
- **Double replacement (metathesis)**: AB + CD → AD + CB
- **Combustion**: hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

### 4. States of Matter and Gas Laws (12%)

#### States of Matter
- **Solid**: definite shape and volume, particles vibrate in fixed positions
- **Liquid**: definite volume, takes shape of container
- **Gas**: no definite shape or volume, particles move freely
- **Phase changes**: melting, freezing, evaporation, condensation, sublimation, deposition
- **Heating curves**: flat sections = phase changes (temperature constant while energy breaks/forms IMFs)

#### Gas Laws
- **Boyle's Law**: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (T constant)
- **Charles's Law**: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (P constant)
- **Gay-Lussac's Law**: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (V constant)
- **Combined Gas Law**: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂
- **Ideal Gas Law**: PV = nRT (R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K or 8.314 J/mol·K)
- **Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures**: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ...
- **Graham's Law of Effusion**: rate₁/rate₂ = sqrt(M₂/M₁)
- **STP**: 0°C (273 K), 1 atm; 1 mol of ideal gas = 22.4 L at STP
- **Always use Kelvin for gas law calculations**: K = °C + 273

#### Kinetic Molecular Theory
- Gas particles are in constant, random motion
- No intermolecular forces between ideal gas particles
- Collisions are perfectly elastic
- Average kinetic energy is proportional to temperature (in Kelvin)

### 5. Solutions (7%)

- **Solute**: substance dissolved; **Solvent**: substance doing the dissolving
- **"Like dissolves like"**: polar dissolves polar; nonpolar dissolves nonpolar
- **Saturation**: saturated, unsaturated, supersaturated solutions
- **Factors affecting solubility**: temperature (solids: more soluble at higher T; gases: less soluble at higher T), pressure (gases: Henry's Law)

#### Concentration Units
- **Molarity (M)**: moles of solute / liters of solution
- **Molality (m)**: moles of solute / kilograms of solvent
- **Percent by mass**: (mass solute / mass solution) x 100
- **Dilution**: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂

#### Colligative Properties
- Depend on number of solute particles, not identity
- **Boiling point elevation**: ΔTb = iKbm
- **Freezing point depression**: ΔTf = iKfm
- **Osmotic pressure**: π = iMRT
- **Van't Hoff factor (i)**: number of particles the solute dissociates into

### 6. Acids and Bases (10%)

#### Definitions
- **Arrhenius**: acids produce H⁺ in water; bases produce OH⁻
- **Bronsted-Lowry**: acids donate protons (H⁺); bases accept protons
- **Lewis**: acids accept electron pairs; bases donate electron pairs

#### pH and Calculations
- **pH = -log[H⁺]** and **[H⁺] = 10^(-pH)**
- **pOH = -log[OH⁻]**
- **pH + pOH = 14** (at 25°C)
- **Strong acids**: completely dissociate (HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄)
- **Strong bases**: completely dissociate (NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂)
- **Weak acids/bases**: partially dissociate; have Ka or Kb values
- **Conjugate acid-base pairs**: differ by one proton

#### Neutralization and Buffers
- **Neutralization**: acid + base → salt + water
- **Titration**: using a solution of known concentration to find an unknown concentration
- **Equivalence point**: moles of acid = moles of base
- **Buffers**: resist pH changes; made of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or weak base and its conjugate acid)
- **Henderson-Hasselbalch**: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])

### 7. Thermodynamics (10%)

- **System vs. surroundings**
- **Exothermic**: releases heat (ΔH < 0); endothermic: absorbs heat (ΔH > 0)
- **Enthalpy (ΔH)**: heat of reaction at constant pressure
- **Hess's Law**: ΔH for a reaction = sum of ΔH for individual steps
- **Calorimetry**: q = mcΔT (q = heat, m = mass, c = specific heat, ΔT = temperature change)
- **Standard enthalpy of formation (ΔHf°)**: ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔHf°(products) - Σ ΔHf°(reactants)
- **Entropy (ΔS)**: measure of disorder; increases with more gas, higher temperature, more particles
- **Gibbs Free Energy**: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS
  - ΔG < 0: spontaneous
  - ΔG > 0: non-spontaneous
  - ΔG = 0: at equilibrium

### 8. Reaction Kinetics (8%)

- **Reaction rate**: change in concentration over time
- **Factors affecting rate**: concentration, temperature, surface area, catalyst
- **Rate law**: rate = k[A]^m[B]^n (determined experimentally)
- **Reaction order**: sum of exponents (m + n); zero, first, second order
- **Activation energy (Ea)**: minimum energy needed for a reaction to occur
- **Catalysts**: lower the activation energy without being consumed
- **Collision theory**: molecules must collide with sufficient energy and proper orientation
- **Reaction coordinate diagrams**: show Ea, ΔH, transition state, intermediates
- **Arrhenius equation**: k = Ae^(-Ea/RT)

#### Chemical Equilibrium
- **Dynamic equilibrium**: forward and reverse rates are equal; concentrations are constant
- **Equilibrium expression**: K = [products]^coefficients / [reactants]^coefficients
- **Kp vs. Kc**: Kp uses partial pressures; Kc uses molar concentrations
- **Le Chatelier's Principle**: a system at equilibrium will shift to counteract a stress
  - Add reactant → shift right; add product → shift left
  - Increase temperature: shift toward endothermic direction
  - Increase pressure: shift toward fewer moles of gas
  - Catalysts do NOT shift equilibrium (reach equilibrium faster)
- **Q vs. K**: Q < K (shift right), Q > K (shift left), Q = K (at equilibrium)

### 9. Electrochemistry (4%)

- **Oxidation**: loss of electrons (OIL); **Reduction**: gain of electrons (RIG)
- **Oxidation numbers**: rules for assigning
- **Balancing redox equations**: half-reaction method
- **Galvanic (voltaic) cells**: spontaneous redox reaction produces electricity (ΔG < 0, E° > 0)
  - Anode: oxidation occurs (negative terminal)
  - Cathode: reduction occurs (positive terminal)
  - Salt bridge: maintains electrical neutrality
- **Electrolytic cells**: non-spontaneous; require external electricity (ΔG > 0, E° < 0)
- **Standard cell potential**: E°cell = E°cathode - E°anode
- **Standard reduction potentials**: more positive = stronger oxidizing agent

## Study Tips

1. Know your gas laws and be able to solve problems quickly.
2. Master stoichiometry — it underlies nearly every calculation on the exam.
3. Understand VSEPR theory and be able to predict molecular shapes from Lewis structures.
4. Practice pH calculations and acid-base problems.
5. Le Chatelier's Principle questions are very common — understand all types of stresses.
6. Don't neglect thermodynamics; know when a reaction is spontaneous using ΔG.
7. Use dimensional analysis (unit cancellation) for all calculations.

## Practice Questions

**1.** What is the electron configuration of a neutral chlorine atom (Z = 17)?

A) 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶
B) 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁵
C) 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3d⁵
D) 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹ 3p⁶

**2.** What is the molecular geometry of water (H₂O)?

A) Linear
B) Trigonal planar
C) Tetrahedral
D) Bent

**3.** How many moles of O₂ are needed to completely combust 2 moles of CH₄?
Reaction: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

A) 1
B) 2
C) 4
D) 8

**4.** A gas occupies 4.0 L at 2.0 atm. If the temperature is held constant and the pressure increases to 4.0 atm, what is the new volume?

A) 8.0 L
B) 2.0 L
C) 1.0 L
D) 16.0 L

**5.** What is the pH of a 0.001 M HCl solution?

A) 1
B) 3
C) 11
D) 7

**6.** According to Le Chatelier's Principle, if the temperature is increased for an exothermic reaction at equilibrium, the equilibrium will:

A) Shift to the right (toward products)
B) Shift to the left (toward reactants)
C) Not change
D) Reach a new equilibrium with a higher K value

**7.** Which intermolecular force is primarily responsible for the high boiling point of water?

A) London dispersion forces
B) Dipole-dipole interactions
C) Hydrogen bonding
D) Ionic bonding

**8.** In a galvanic cell, oxidation occurs at the:

A) Cathode
B) Anode
C) Salt bridge
D) External wire

**9.** A reaction has ΔH = -100 kJ and ΔS = +50 J/K. At 298 K, is this reaction spontaneous?

A) Yes, because ΔG is negative
B) No, because ΔG is positive
C) Only at higher temperatures
D) Cannot be determined

**10.** If the rate law for a reaction is rate = k[A]²[B], what is the overall order of the reaction?

A) 1
B) 2
C) 3
D) 4

## Answer Key

1. **B) 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁵** — Chlorine has 17 electrons. Fill orbitals in order: 2+2+6+2+5 = 17.
2. **D) Bent** — Water has 4 electron groups (2 bonding, 2 lone pairs) around oxygen. The molecular geometry (based on atoms only) is bent, with a bond angle of about 104.5°.
3. **C) 4** — The balanced equation shows 1 mol CH₄ requires 2 mol O₂. So 2 mol CH₄ requires 2 x 2 = 4 mol O₂.
4. **B) 2.0 L** — Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂. (2.0)(4.0) = (4.0)(V₂). V₂ = 2.0 L.
5. **B) 3** — HCl is a strong acid; it fully dissociates. [H⁺] = 0.001 = 10⁻³. pH = -log(10⁻³) = 3.
6. **B) Shift to the left** — For an exothermic reaction, heat is a product. Adding heat (increasing temperature) shifts equilibrium toward reactants.
7. **C) Hydrogen bonding** — Water molecules form hydrogen bonds (H bonded to O), which are much stronger than regular dipole-dipole forces.
8. **B) Anode** — Remember "An Ox, Red Cat" — Anode = Oxidation, Cathode = Reduction.
9. **A) Yes, because ΔG is negative** — ΔG = ΔH - TΔS = -100,000 - (298)(50) = -100,000 - 14,900 = -114,900 J. Negative ΔG = spontaneous. (Note: convert ΔH to J or ΔS to kJ.)
10. **C) 3** — Overall order = sum of exponents = 2 + 1 = 3.
