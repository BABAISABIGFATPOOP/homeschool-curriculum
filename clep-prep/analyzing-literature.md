# Analyzing and Interpreting Literature CLEP Study Guide

## Exam Overview

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Format** | Multiple choice |
| **Number of Questions** | ~80 |
| **Time Limit** | 90 minutes |
| **Passing Score** | ~50 out of 80 (scaled); approximately 50% correct |
| **College Credit** | Typically 6 semester hours |
| **Note** | Passages are provided on the exam; no prior reading list is required |

This exam tests your ability to read and analyze literary works across genres (poetry, prose fiction, drama, and nonfiction). You do NOT need to have read specific works — all passages are provided. The key skill is close reading and interpretation.

## Topics Covered

### 1. Poetry Analysis (15-20%)

#### Form and Structure
- **Stanza types**: couplet (2 lines), tercet (3), quatrain (4), sestet (6), octave (8)
- **Sonnet**: Shakespearean (3 quatrains + couplet, ABAB CDCD EFEF GG) vs. Petrarchan (octave + sestet, ABBAABBA + CDECDE)
- **Free verse**: no regular meter or rhyme scheme
- **Blank verse**: unrhymed iambic pentameter
- **Ode, elegy, ballad, haiku, limerick, epic**
- **Rhyme scheme**: end rhyme, internal rhyme, slant rhyme
- **Enjambment**: a line that continues without pause into the next
- **Caesura**: a pause within a line of poetry

#### Meter and Rhythm
- **Foot types**: iamb (da-DUM), trochee (DUM-da), anapest (da-da-DUM), dactyl (DUM-da-da), spondee (DUM-DUM)
- **Line lengths**: monometer, dimeter, trimeter, tetrameter, pentameter, hexameter
- **Iambic pentameter**: five iambs per line (most common in English poetry)
- **Scanning poetry**: marking stressed and unstressed syllables

#### Figurative Language
- **Simile**: comparison using "like" or "as"
- **Metaphor**: direct comparison without "like" or "as"
- **Extended metaphor**: sustained metaphor throughout a passage or poem
- **Personification**: giving human qualities to non-human things
- **Apostrophe**: addressing an absent person, abstract concept, or object
- **Hyperbole**: deliberate exaggeration
- **Understatement/Litotes**: deliberate minimizing
- **Synecdoche**: part represents the whole ("all hands on deck")
- **Metonymy**: related term substitutes for the thing itself ("the crown" for the monarchy)
- **Oxymoron**: contradictory terms together ("bitter sweet")
- **Paradox**: seemingly contradictory statement that reveals a truth

#### Sound Devices
- **Alliteration**: repetition of initial consonant sounds
- **Assonance**: repetition of vowel sounds
- **Consonance**: repetition of consonant sounds (usually internal or final)
- **Onomatopoeia**: words that imitate sounds

#### Interpreting Poetry
- Identify the speaker (not necessarily the poet)
- Determine tone and mood
- Look for shifts in tone, subject, or perspective (volta in sonnets)
- Consider the title as a clue to meaning
- Paraphrase difficult lines before interpreting

### 2. Prose Fiction (45-50%)

#### Character
- **Protagonist vs. antagonist**
- **Round characters**: complex, multi-dimensional
- **Flat characters**: one-dimensional, defined by a single trait
- **Static vs. dynamic**: dynamic characters change; static do not
- **Direct vs. indirect characterization**: told about vs. shown through actions, speech, thoughts
- **Foil**: a character that contrasts with another to highlight traits
- **Motivation**: what drives a character's actions

#### Theme
- **Theme vs. subject**: subject is the topic; theme is the message or insight about that topic
- A theme is a complete statement (not just "love" but "love requires sacrifice")
- **Universal themes**: good vs. evil, coming of age, identity, mortality, power, isolation
- Themes are revealed through plot, characters, symbols, and conflict
- A work can have multiple themes

#### Setting
- **Time and place**: historical period, geographic location, time of day, season
- **Atmosphere/mood**: the emotional feeling created by the setting
- Setting can reflect or contrast with characters' inner states
- Setting as symbol (e.g., a decaying house representing moral decline)
- Social and cultural context as part of setting

#### Narrative Perspective
- **First person**: narrator is a character ("I"); limited to their perceptions
- **Third person limited**: narrator focuses on one character's thoughts
- **Third person omniscient**: narrator knows all characters' thoughts
- **Second person**: "you" (rare, used for effect)
- **Unreliable narrator**: a narrator whose credibility is compromised
- **Stream of consciousness**: continuous flow of a character's thoughts

#### Plot and Structure
- **Exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution**
- **Conflict types**: person vs. person, person vs. self, person vs. society, person vs. nature, person vs. technology, person vs. fate
- **Foreshadowing**: hints about future events
- **Flashback**: a scene from the past inserted into the narrative
- **In medias res**: beginning in the middle of the action
- **Irony**: dramatic (audience knows something characters don't), situational (outcome differs from expectation), verbal (saying the opposite of what is meant)

### 3. Drama (15-20%)

#### Dialogue
- Dialogue is the primary vehicle for characterization in drama
- **Monologue**: a long speech by one character to others on stage
- **Soliloquy**: a speech where a character reveals thoughts to the audience (alone on stage)
- **Aside**: a brief remark to the audience that other characters don't hear
- Subtext: what is implied but not directly stated in dialogue

#### Staging and Structure
- **Stage directions**: indicate movement, tone, setting
- **Acts and scenes**: structural divisions
- **Tragedy**: protagonist's downfall due to a flaw (hamartia) or fate
- **Comedy**: lighthearted, often ends in marriage or resolution
- **Tragicomedy**: blends elements of both
- **Dramatic irony**: the audience knows something the characters do not
- **Chorus**: in Greek drama, a group that comments on the action

#### Conflict in Drama
- Internal conflict revealed through soliloquy and dialogue
- External conflict drives the plot forward
- **Tragic flaw (hamartia)**: the character trait leading to the protagonist's downfall (e.g., Hamlet's indecision, Macbeth's ambition)
- **Catharsis**: the emotional release the audience experiences
- **Recognition (anagnorisis)**: a character's moment of critical discovery

### 4. Literary Terms and Devices

#### Tone and Style
- **Tone**: the author's attitude toward the subject (e.g., satirical, somber, playful, ironic)
- **Mood**: the emotional atmosphere felt by the reader
- **Diction**: word choice — formal, informal, colloquial, archaic, technical
- **Syntax**: sentence structure — long complex sentences vs. short declarative ones
- **Voice**: the distinctive style of the author or narrator

#### Symbolism and Allegory
- **Symbol**: something that represents something beyond its literal meaning
- **Allegory**: an extended narrative where characters and events represent abstract ideas (e.g., Orwell's Animal Farm)
- **Motif**: a recurring element (image, idea, phrase) that develops a theme

#### Other Important Terms
- **Allusion**: a reference to another work, person, or event
- **Satire**: using humor, irony, or exaggeration to criticize
- **Imagery**: vivid sensory language appealing to sight, sound, taste, touch, smell
- **Point of view vs. perspective**: POV is the grammatical person; perspective is the worldview
- **Juxtaposition**: placing two things side by side for comparison or contrast
- **Epiphany**: a sudden moment of insight or revelation for a character
- **Archetype**: a universal symbol or character type (e.g., the hero's journey, the trickster)
- **Deus ex machina**: an unexpected power or event that resolves an otherwise unresolvable situation

## Study Tips

1. This is a close-reading exam — practice reading passages carefully and identifying literary devices.
2. Focus on poetry analysis skills since many students find poetry the hardest section.
3. Always consider context: how does the passage's meaning change if you consider the tone, speaker, or setting?
4. For every passage, ask: Who is speaking? What is the tone? What is the theme? What devices are used?
5. Read broadly from different periods (Renaissance, Romantic, Victorian, Modern, Contemporary).
6. Pay special attention to the answer choices — eliminate obviously wrong answers first.
7. Don't overthink; the exam rewards careful reading, not obscure knowledge.

## Practice Questions

**1.** In the line "The fog comes on little cat feet," what literary device is primarily used?

A) Simile
B) Metaphor
C) Personification
D) Hyperbole

**2.** A character who contrasts with the protagonist to highlight the protagonist's traits is called a:

A) Antagonist
B) Foil
C) Round character
D) Stock character

**3.** Which point of view uses "I" as the narrator?

A) Third person omniscient
B) Second person
C) First person
D) Third person limited

**4.** In a Shakespearean sonnet, where does the volta (turn) typically occur?

A) At the end of the first quatrain
B) At the beginning of the final couplet
C) At the start of the poem
D) In the middle of the second quatrain

**5.** When a reader knows a character is about to walk into a trap, but the character does not, this is an example of:

A) Verbal irony
B) Situational irony
C) Dramatic irony
D) Cosmic irony

**6.** "The pen is mightier than the sword" is an example of:

A) Metonymy
B) Simile
C) Alliteration
D) Onomatopoeia

**7.** A long speech in a play where a character speaks their thoughts aloud while alone on stage is called a:

A) Monologue
B) Aside
C) Soliloquy
D) Dialogue

**8.** Which term describes a recurring image or idea that develops a theme throughout a literary work?

A) Symbol
B) Motif
C) Allegory
D) Allusion

**9.** The emotional cleansing or release an audience feels at the end of a tragedy is called:

A) Hamartia
B) Anagnorisis
C) Hubris
D) Catharsis

**10.** An unreliable narrator is best described as a narrator who:

A) Tells the story in third person
B) Has a limited perspective that may distort the truth
C) Uses complex vocabulary
D) Speaks directly to the audience

---

## Answer Key

1. **B) Metaphor** — The fog IS a cat (implied comparison); it does not use "like" or "as" (which would be simile). While it gives animal qualities to fog, the core device is metaphor.
2. **B) Foil** — A foil is specifically a character whose contrasting qualities highlight the traits of another character.
3. **C) First person** — First person uses "I" (or "we") as the narrator.
4. **B) At the beginning of the final couplet** — In a Shakespearean sonnet, the couplet (lines 13-14) typically presents a twist or resolution.
5. **C) Dramatic irony** — The audience knows something the character does not.
6. **A) Metonymy** — "Pen" stands for writing/ideas, "sword" stands for military force. These are related concepts substituting for broader ideas.
7. **C) Soliloquy** — A soliloquy is spoken alone on stage, revealing inner thoughts. A monologue is addressed to other characters.
8. **B) Motif** — A motif is a recurring element that reinforces themes. A symbol represents something else; a motif is about pattern and repetition.
9. **D) Catharsis** — Aristotle described catharsis as the purging of emotions (pity and fear) through tragedy.
10. **B) Has a limited perspective that may distort the truth** — An unreliable narrator's account cannot be fully trusted due to bias, immaturity, mental instability, or deception.
